## TIER 1 — Hard factual errors that must be fixed

### 1. Kirk Bryan Sr. died in WYOMING, not Colorado.
**Post says** (paragraph 4): "his son Kirk Bryan Sr. was a Harvard geology professor who... died suddenly of a heart attack on a field trip in Colorado in August 1950."

**Verified fact**: Kirk Bryan Sr. died **22 August 1950 in Cody, Wyoming**, of a heart attack while on a field trip with archaeologists. (Source: Wikipedia "Kirk Bryan (geologist)" and the AGU memoir of Bryan Sr.) Cody, Wyoming, not Colorado.

**Suggested fix**: change "Colorado" → "Wyoming". (The "field trip" and "heart attack" details are correct.)

### 2. Michael Cox was 12 YEARS YOUNGER than Bryan, not "a year older."
**Post says** (section "The Mike Cox Subplot", paragraph 1): "He had no scientific training and no PhD. He was a year older than Bryan, blue-collar in background..."

**Verified fact**: Bryan was born **21 July 1929**; Cox's dates are **1941-1989**. Cox was **12 years YOUNGER** than Bryan, not older. (Source: Bryan 1991 *J. Phys. Oceanogr.* tribute paper title "Michael Cox (1941-1989)"; Wikipedia confirms Bryan 1929.)

**Suggested fix**: change "He was a year older than Bryan" → "He was twelve years younger than Bryan" (or simply: "He was in his early twenties").

The "blue-collar in background" claim is unsourced and should be removed or softened. The Bryan 1991 tribute notes that Cox was a religious conscientious objector and chose the U.S. Weather Bureau's General Circulation Laboratory in 1962 partly because it was consistent with his beliefs — that's the documented motivation, not "blue-collar background."

### 3. Cox joined GFDL in 1962, and his first job was on the IBM Stretch.
**Post says** (section "Adding the Density Field"): "The Cox in the co-author line was a young man who had arrived at GFDL not as a scientist but as a computer operator..."
**Post says** ("The Mike Cox Subplot"): "Cox arrived at GFDL in the early 1960s as a computer operator. His job was to run the overnight batch queue: mount tapes, load card decks..."

**Verified fact**: Bryan's 1991 tribute states explicitly: "In 1962 Cox chose to work at the U.S. Weather Bureau's General Circulation Laboratory (now NOAA's Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory)... His first job was operating the IBM 'Stretch' computer."

**Suggested fix**: change "in the early 1960s" → "in 1962" (and optionally name the IBM Stretch explicitly).

Also: in 1973-74 Cox attended the University of Washington as a graduate student in oceanography. The post's blanket "no scientific training" overlooks this. It is true Cox never completed a PhD, but he did formally study oceanography. Consider adding a parenthetical: "(Cox would later, in 1973-74, attend the University of Washington as a graduate student in oceanography, though he did not complete a PhD.)"

### 4. MOM1 was released in 1991, not December 1990.
**Post says** (section "The Lineage"): "The rewrite was released in December 1990 as **MOM1**, the first 'Modular Ocean Model.'"

**Verified fact**: The GFDL MOM history paper (Griffies et al. 2015/2017) explicitly says "MOM1 (1991)" and cites: "Pacanowski, R.C., K.W. Dixon, and A. Rosati, 1991: The G.F.D.L. Modular Ocean Model User's Guide. GFDL Ocean Technical Report No. 2."

**Suggested fix**: change "December 1990" → "1991". (If Dec 1990 corresponds to an internal pre-release that the post is privileging, the source for that specific date is not in the public MOM history; safest to follow the published release date.)

### 5. The Alexander Agassiz Medal recipient list named in the post is partially fabricated.
**Post says** (section "Princeton, 2023"): "and its recipients since 1913 include Walter Munk, Henry Stommel, Carl Wunsch, Jorge Sarmiento, and Manabe's old GFDL director Joseph Smagorinsky."

**Verified fact**: Walter Munk (1976) and Henry Stommel (1979) are confirmed Agassiz Medal recipients. **Carl Wunsch, Jorge Sarmiento, and Joseph Smagorinsky do NOT appear on the list of Agassiz Medal recipients** (per Wikipedia's complete list and the NAS award page). 
- Smagorinsky received the Carl-Gustaf Rossby Research Medal (1972), the IMO Prize (1974), the Buys Ballot Medal (1973), and the Benjamin Franklin Medal (2003) — but not the Agassiz.
- Wunsch received the Maurice Ewing Medal (1990), the William Bowie Medal (2006), and others — but not the Agassiz.
- Sarmiento — not found on the Agassiz list.

**Suggested fix**: Replace with verified recipients. Confirmed Agassiz recipients in the post's "modern" range include Walter Munk (1976), Henry Stommel (1979), Wallace Broecker (1986), Joseph Reid (1992), Klaus Wyrtki (2004), Sallie Chisholm (2010), David Karl (2013), Dean Roemmich (2018), Kirk Bryan (2023). Recommended replacement: "and its recipients since 1913 include Walter Munk, Henry Stommel, Wallace Broecker, and Sallie Chisholm" (or similar — keep two or three names that are actually confirmed).

### 6. The Bryan-Gill 1971 paper title in footnote [^20] is wrong.
**Post says** (footnote 20): "Bryan, K., and Gill, A. E. (1971). 'A note on the heat transport by fluctuating currents.' An earlier joint paper on Southern Ocean dynamics with Adrian Gill during Gill's sabbatical at GFDL."

**Verified fact**: The actual Gill & Bryan 1971 paper is "Effects of geometry on the circulation of a three-dimensional southern-hemisphere ocean model," **Deep-Sea Research vol. 18, pp. 685-721** (author order: Gill, A.E., and K. Bryan). The title "A note on the heat transport by fluctuating currents" appears to be from a different paper (possibly Bryan 1962, *J. Geophys. Res.*).

**Suggested fix**: Replace footnote 20 with the correct title and citation:
> "Gill, A. E., and Bryan, K. (1971). 'Effects of geometry on the circulation of a three-dimensional southern-hemisphere ocean model.' *Deep-Sea Research* 18, 685-721. Collaborative paper from Adrian Gill's sabbatical at GFDL."

### 7. The 1969 coupled model used a 120°-longitude sector, not "realistic North Pacific and North Atlantic geometry."
**Post says** (section "Four Pages, Half a Megabyte"): "The ocean component was Bryan's five-level model, running with a rigid lid and realistic North Pacific and North Atlantic geometry."

**Verified fact**: NOAA's 200th-anniversary retrospective on the Manabe-Bryan 1969 paper states the coupled model "covered only one-sixth of the Earth's surface, from the pole to the equator and 120° in longitude" — i.e., a single 120° longitude sector running from the equator to the pole, with an idealized continent at one boundary. It was not a realistic two-ocean geometry.

**Suggested fix**: change "realistic North Pacific and North Atlantic geometry" → "an idealized sector geometry covering 120° of longitude from the equator to the pole" (or similar). The fully global realistic-geometry coupled model came later, in the 1970s.

## TIER 2 — Smaller corrections / clarifications

### 8. Stommel's career: post omits Harvard.
**Post says** (section "The Stommel Bracket"): "Stommel spent most of his career at Woods Hole. He taught briefly at MIT in the 1960s, returned to Woods Hole in 1978..."

**Verified fact**: Stommel was at Woods Hole 1944-1959, **Harvard 1959-1963**, MIT 1963-1978, returned to Woods Hole 1978-1992.

**Suggested fix**: change "He taught briefly at MIT in the 1960s" → "He moved to Harvard in 1959, then to MIT in 1963, returning to Woods Hole in 1978." (More accurate without adding much length.)

### 9. The Stommel 1948 paper page count.
**Post says** (paragraph after WHOI image): "He had published, in 1948, a short paper in the *Transactions of the American Geophysical Union*... In three pages and one equation, Stommel explained..."

**Verified fact**: The paper occupies pages 202-206 in *Trans. AGU* 29(2), i.e., five page numbers. The text itself is short, but "three pages" is a generous count.

**Suggested fix**: change "In three pages and one equation" → "In a five-page note containing one central equation" — or simply "In a short paper with one central equation."

---

### 10. CDC 6600 delivery year.
**Post says** (section "Adding the Density Field"): "The computer was a CDC 6600, which Control Data had begun delivering in 1964..."

**Verified fact**: The CDC 6600 was introduced in September 1964 and the first production units were delivered to Livermore and Los Alamos in 1965. Lawrence Livermore did receive serial #1 in September 1964, so "began delivering in 1964" is technically defensible but the broad community received units starting 1965. Minor issue.

**Suggested fix** (optional): change "had begun delivering in 1964" → "had introduced in 1964 (with the first production deliveries in 1964-1965)." Or leave as is — within tolerance.

---

### 11. Adcroft and Hallberg are not the only MOM6 authors.
**Post says** (section "The Lineage"): "The current version at GFDL is **MOM6**, released in 2019 by Alistair Adcroft and Robert Hallberg."

**Verified fact**: The 2019 description paper "The GFDL global ocean and sea ice model OM4.0: Model description and simulation features" lists Adcroft, Anderson, Balaji, and many other co-authors. MOM6 itself was the result of community development over years preceding the 2019 paper.

**Suggested fix**: change "by Alistair Adcroft and Robert Hallberg" → "led by Alistair Adcroft and Robert Hallberg with many collaborators" — or just remove the two-name attribution.
