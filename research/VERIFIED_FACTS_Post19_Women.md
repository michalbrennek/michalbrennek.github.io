## DISCREPANCY 1: Trajectory time
Post says: "20 seconds — a problem that took a human computer 40 hours to solve by hand"
Wikipedia ENIAC: "trajectory of a missile in 15 seconds, a task that would have taken several weeks for a human computer"
WebSearch summary: alternative reports of "30 seconds" / "20 hours for 60-second trajectory" / "3 days"

Note: The "20 seconds" figure is commonly cited in Kleiman's book "Proving Ground" (2022). The "40 hours" figure does appear in some Kleiman/EPP materials in reference to a single trajectory taking 40 hours by hand. So although Wikipedia disagrees, the post's specific pairing matches Kleiman's account. Mark as: cited and defensible to source, but reviewers should note Wikipedia inconsistency. NOT a clear error.

## DISCREPANCY 2: Klara von Neumann + 1950 weather forecast (CRITICAL)
Post says: "She worked for 32 days on the project, managing 100 000 punch cards. A 2021 Smithsonian correction later clarified that her primary contribution was writing and checking the code and training the meteorologists to program the ENIAC -- she was not merely a punch-card operator, as earlier accounts had implied. She was the programmer."

ISSUES:
1. The "32 days" actually refers to the 1948 Monte Carlo work (Apr 1948: 32 days straight), NOT the 1950 weather forecast (which took ~33 days according to one source). The post conflates two separate events.
2. The "100,000 punch cards" claim was explicitly corrected by Smithsonian (per their editor's note): "in fact she wasn't present for this part of the experiment."
3. The 2021 Smithsonian correction actually went the OPPOSITE direction from the post's framing. The correction DOWNGRADED Klara's role on the weather forecast — historian Tom Haigh found "absolutely no evidence" she wrote or ran the weather forecast code. Her documented role was advising on coding technique and checking the final code (as stated in 1950 paper acknowledgments).
4. The post says "She was the programmer" — this is the very claim Smithsonian had to retract. The actual programmers/operators were ballistics lab staff (possibly Hedi Selberg and Margaret Smagorinsky).

RECOMMENDED REWRITE: Klara's documented contribution to the 1950 weather forecast was teaching the ENIAC coding technique and checking the final code. She was the world's leading expert on ENIAC coding by then — but the operators in 1950 were others. (Save the "32 days / 100,000 cards" detail for the 1948 Monte Carlo work, where it actually belongs.)

## DISCREPANCY 3: ENIAC unveiling date
- Press conference/announcement to public: evening of Feb 14, 1946
- Formal dedication ceremony: Feb 15, 1946
- Post says "February 15, 1946 ... unveiling ENIAC ... to the press and the public at the Moore School"
- The Feb 15 was the formal dedication; the press demo was Feb 14 evening. The post is technically defensible as referring to the formal unveiling day, but readers should note both dates have been cited.
