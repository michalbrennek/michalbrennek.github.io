## VERIFIED CORRECT

### Frank Rosenblatt biography
- **Born July 11, 1928, New Rochelle, NY** -- confirmed Wikipedia + Cornell Faculty Memorial Statement (eCommons PDF, Stephen T. Emlen, Howard C. Howland, Richard D. O'Brien).
- **Died July 11, 1971 (his 43rd birthday), drowning in Chesapeake Bay** -- Cornell Faculty Memorial confirms "Sunday afternoon, July 11, 1971, in a boating accident in Chesapeake Bay." UMass page confirms "drowning on his 43rd birthday."
- **Graduated Bronx High School of Science, 1946** -- confirmed Wikipedia.
- **Cornell A.B. 1950, Ph.D. 1956** -- confirmed Cornell Faculty Memorial.
- **Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory in Buffalo, NY (research psychologist)** -- confirmed Cornell Faculty Memorial.
- **Joined Cornell Ithaca campus 1959 as director of Cognitive Systems Research Program** -- Cornell Faculty Memorial.
- **In 1971 was acting (co-)chair of Section of Neurobiology and Behavior** -- Cornell Faculty Memorial: "in 1971 he shared the acting chairmanship of the Section of Neurobiology and Behavior."
- **Active in McCarthy primary campaigns, Vietnam protests** -- Cornell Faculty Memorial: "He was active in the McCarthy primary campaigns in New Hampshire and California, in Democratic reform politics in New York State, and in a series of Vietnam protest activities in Washington."
- **Memory transfer through brain extracts research** -- confirmed Cornell Faculty Memorial: "transfer of learned behavior from trained to naive rats by the injection of brain extracts."
- **SETI / planet-detection research** -- Cornell Faculty Memorial confirms astronomical research. Nagy 2011 confirms NASA grant for Stellar Coherometer. Rosenblatt 1971 published "A two-color photometric method for detection of extra-solar planetary systems" in *Icarus*.
- **Marvin Minsky one year ahead at Bronx Science** -- Cornell Chronicle confirms "same school as Marvin Minsky, one grade ahead." Minsky born Aug 9 1927, Rosenblatt July 11 1928 -- 13 months apart, supports one-year-ahead claim.

### Project PARA / The Perceptron
- **"Project PARA" = Perceiving and Recognizing Automaton, Cornell Aeronautical Lab, January 1957** -- confirmed via Nagy 2011 slides ("The Perceptron, A Perceiving and Recognizing Automaton (Project Para), CAL Rept. No. 85-460-1, January 1957"); also Biblio.com listing of the actual report.
- **Simulated on IBM 704** -- confirmed Wikipedia + Cornell Chronicle.
- **1958 paper: "The Perceptron: A Probabilistic Model for Information Storage and Organization in the Brain," Psychological Review 65(6)** -- confirmed via Nagy 2011.

### Mark I Perceptron hardware
- **400 photocells in 20x20 grid (sensory/S-units)** -- confirmed Wikipedia Perceptron article + Nagy 2011 slides ("Mark I: 400 S-units").
- **512 association units (A-units)** -- confirmed Wikipedia + Nagy 2011 ("512 A-units").
- **8 response units (R-units)** -- confirmed Wikipedia + Nagy 2011 ("8 R-units").
- **Weights = potentiometers, learning via electric motors** -- confirmed Wikipedia Perceptron.
- **Funded by Office of Naval Research** -- confirmed Wikipedia + Nagy 2011 ("Most of the ONR funding went to machine construction").
- **Now in Smithsonian National Museum of American History** -- confirmed Nagy 2011 + Cornell Chronicle.
- **Publicly demonstrated June 23, 1960** -- Wikipedia Perceptron. (Post says "constructed in 1960" -- compatible.)
- **99.8% accuracy on shapes; 100% squares vs diamonds; 100% X vs E** -- Wikipedia Mark I Perceptron article confirms all three figures.

### NYT 1958 press conference
- **July 7, 1958: ONR press conference in Washington (UPI dispatch); story ran July 8 NYT page 25** -- AITopics confirms dateline "Washington, July 7 (UPI)" + page 25 + July 8 publication.
- **"Embryo of an electronic computer ... able to walk, talk, see, write, reproduce itself and be conscious of its existence"** -- exact wording confirmed by AITopics archive.
- **Rosenblatt's quote: "the first machine which is capable of having an original idea"** -- confirmed Cornell Chronicle.
- **Rosenblatt on assembly-line reproduction quote** -- attributed in NYT article (per AITopics summary).

## NEEDS QUALIFICATION OR MINOR CORRECTION

### Telescope size
- Post: "**12-inch** Cassegrain telescope"
- UMass page: "**Fecker 12-1/2"** Cassegrain"
- Minor: should be 12.5 inch (or "12.5-inch") not 12-inch.

### AlexNet runner-up error rate
- Post: "**26.2%**"
- ImageNet official results: runner-up (ISI team) was **0.26172 = 26.172%** which rounds to **26.2%**. So the post is correct (rounded to one decimal).
- Wikipedia phrases the gap as "more than 10.8% above the runner-up" which is consistent with 26.1-26.2%. **No correction needed.**

### "X and E with 100% accuracy after just 20 training examples"
- Post: "could classify the letters X and E with 100% accuracy after just 20 training examples"
- Wikipedia confirms 100% accuracy on X and E (with limited rotation) but does NOT mention "20 training examples" specifically. The figure may originate from a specific Mark I demo write-up but is not corroborated by the standard sources. **Either provide a citation for the "20 examples" figure or remove it.**

### Mansfield Amendment year
- Post: "**Mansfield Amendment of 1969**" required DARPA-funded research to have **military** applications
- Wikipedia AI Winter says Mansfield Amendment shifted DARPA priorities to "mission-oriented direct research, rather than basic undirected research." Whether the word "military" vs "mission-oriented" matches is a matter of nuance: the amendment did apply to defense department funding and required relevance to "military function or operation." So "military applications" is fair shorthand but slightly tighter than the original wording.

### Minsky-Papert hidden-layer quote attribution (footnote [^3])
- Post footnote [^3] cites "Minsky, M. & Papert, S. (1969). *Perceptrons*, MIT Press, **p. 232**." for "Virtually nothing is known about the computational capabilities of this latter kind of machine."
- Wikipedia *Perceptrons (book)* attributes this passage to the **1971 Report of Project MAC** by Minsky and Papert (paraphrased in the 1988 expanded edition prologue).
- The quote may also appear in the 1969 book -- it is widely cited from both -- but page 232 of the 1969 first edition has not been verified by me. **Action: either (a) verify the page number from a copy of the 1969 book, or (b) reword the citation to the 1988 expanded edition where Wikipedia confirms the quote appears.**
