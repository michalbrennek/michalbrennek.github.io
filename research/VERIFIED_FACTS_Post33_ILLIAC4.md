## Errors / Suggested Corrections
- **Post says Slotnick worked at Westinghouse in Pittsburgh**. Multiple sources (Wikipedia, search-result summary citing the History.Computer.org IEEE Profile) confirm Slotnick was at Westinghouse Electric Corporation in **Baltimore, Maryland** (not Pittsburgh). It is notable that Slotnick died in Baltimore in 1985 — he may have been visiting old colleagues. This is a clear factual error.
- **Post says Slotnick died at age 54**. He was 53 (born 12 Nov 1931, died 25 Oct 1985 — 18 days short of his 54th birthday). Wikipedia: "age 53". The post should say 53.
- **Post says Robert Fassnacht was "a thirty-three-year-old physicist working overnight on a thesis-related experiment"**. Fassnacht was a postdoctoral researcher (Ph.D. already complete) in superconductivity. He was cooling a dewar with liquid nitrogen before leaving for a family vacation in San Diego. Suggest: "postdoctoral physicist working overnight on a superconductivity experiment before a family vacation".
- **Post says Hans Mark "emigrated to the United States in 1940"**. He left Germany in late 1939 for Canada and arrived in the US around a year later (settling in Brooklyn). The "1940" framing is broadly defensible but glosses over the Canadian stopover. Optional: "emigrated from Germany in late 1939, settling in the United States by 1940".
- **Post's sentence about Mark's career**: "took his doctorate in physics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1954, and held positions at Berkeley, Lawrence Livermore (where he worked under and befriended Edward Teller), and Stanford before being appointed Director of NASA Ames". Wikipedia says he took BS Physics at UC Berkeley in 1951 BEFORE the MIT PhD; he was at LLNL 1955-1958 then 1960-1964 (research physicist, then head of Experimental Physics Division). Stanford came later. The post's ordering is OK but compresses chronology. **No correction strictly required** — though the Berkeley reference being to his undergraduate degree may not be obvious.
- **Connection Machine CM-1 year**: Post claims "Connection Machine CM-1 (1985)". Wikipedia article body gives CM-1 introduction year as 1986 (in the product timeline). A photo caption gives "(1985)". Hillis dissertation 1985. **Ambiguous but acceptable**; consider noting both years or using "1985-1986" if the strict shipping/availability date matters.

## Errors / Suggested Corrections (continued)
- **Slotnick's IAS Electronic Computer Project (1952-1954/56) under von Neumann is omitted**. The post jumps from his master's at NYU directly to IBM Poughkeepsie 1957. Source: history.computer.org and several biographical references state he joined the IAS staff in June 1952 after his Columbia MS, where he raised the parallel-computing idea to von Neumann (who dismissed it as "too many tubes"); this is a load-bearing historical detail since the post repeatedly invokes IAS for other characters in the series, and the von Neumann "too many tubes" anecdote is a famous origin story for parallel computing.
- **Slotnick's Columbia MS 1952 (not NYU MA)**. Post claims "his Master of Arts and Doctor of Philosophy in mathematics at New York University". Multiple sources: BA Columbia 1951, MS Columbia 1952, then PhD applied math at NYU Courant 1956. NYU was only his doctorate, not master's. (Note: some sources say his PhD was 1956 from NYU Courant; Math Genealogy Project says 1957. Slight ambiguity in year. But the MA→NYU framing is wrong.)
- **Slotnick's PhD in "mathematics" at NYU**. His NYU PhD was in **applied mathematics** (Courant Institute), not pure mathematics. Minor accuracy point.
- **Yale Mintz "moved to GISS in the late 1960s"** is incorrect. Mintz remained at UCLA throughout his career; what moved to GISS was the second-generation Mintz-Arakawa UCLA model, adopted by GISS in **1972** (one source confirms; pne.people.si.umich.edu "A Vast Machine" / Edwards's history). The post's framing has Mintz physically transferring east, which is wrong. Suggest rewriting: "The Mintz-Arakawa second-generation UCLA model was adopted at GISS in 1972; James Hansen would later inherit and extend it."

## Open Questions
- Clock speed: Wikipedia says final operational rate was 13 MHz (after 16 MHz design rev from original 25 MHz); post says "16 MHz". Post is citing the designed/post-redesign rate. Hord 1982 may be the post's source; defensible but a footnote could clarify the operational rate was 13 MHz in practice.
- Connection Machine CM-1 year: Hillis dissertation 1985 vs. CM-1 product timeline 1986. Post says "(1985)". Acceptable but the CM-1 commercial introduction was 1986. Either is defensible.
- The 1976 chassis description ("ten feet high, eight feet deep, fifty feet long") is from Hord 1982; not independently re-verified here but the figures are plausible for a single-quadrant ILLIAC IV cabinet.
- The post's footnote 27 says Slotnick "died 25 October 1985 in Baltimore Maryland of an apparent heart attack while jogging, age 54". Wikipedia explicitly says **age 53**.
