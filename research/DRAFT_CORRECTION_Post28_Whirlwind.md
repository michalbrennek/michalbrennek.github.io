## CORRECTION 1 (REAL FACTUAL ERROR -- recommend fix)

**Mina Rees was an algebraist, not a topologist.**

Current text (paragraph beginning "The mathematical sciences division of ONR..."):

> The mathematical sciences division of ONR was headed from February 1949 by **Mina Rees**, a topologist who had spent the war administering applied mathematics for the Office of Scientific Research and Development.

Issue: Rees's PhD (University of Chicago, 1931) was on "Division algebras associated with an equation whose group has four generators" -- a problem in abstract algebra, not topology. Her published work and CV consistently identify her as an algebraist.

Recommended fix:

> The mathematical sciences division of ONR was headed from February 1949 by **Mina Rees**, an algebraist who had spent the war administering applied mathematics for the Office of Scientific Research and Development.

## CORRECTION 2 (MINOR -- consider clarifying)

**Whirlwind storage-tube access time: post says "twenty-five microseconds, four times slower than spec"; Wikipedia gives 30 microseconds.**

Current text (paragraph beginning "The architecture of Whirlwind I..."):

> The original 1947 design specification called for memory access in six microseconds; the storage tubes Whirlwind actually got delivered roughly twenty-five microseconds, four times slower than spec, and they kept failing.

The "twenty-five microseconds" figure is consistent with Papian's later comparison (storage tube access "approximately 25 microseconds"). Wikipedia's Whirlwind I article gives "30 microseconds" (5x spec). Both numbers appear in the historical record. The post is choosing the lower value cited by Papian. This is defensible and probably need not be changed; but if a hedge is desired:

> The original 1947 design specification called for memory access in six microseconds; the storage tubes Whirlwind actually got delivered approximately twenty-five microseconds (some sources give thirty), four times slower than spec, and they kept failing.

Probably not worth the awkwardness. Recommend: leave as-is.

## CORRECTION 3 (MINOR -- consider hedging)

**SAGE cost vs Manhattan Project comparison: "four to six times" in post; Wikipedia says "four times".**

Current text (paragraph beginning "The total program cost across the deployment cycle..."):

> The Manhattan Project cost two billion dollars in WWII-era dollars. SAGE was four to six times the Manhattan Project.

Wikipedia's SAGE article says "four times the cost of the Manhattan Project" with the $8-12 billion cost range stated. The arithmetic of $8-12B / $2B does give 4 to 6, so the post is mathematically defensible. The Wikipedia 4x figure is the lower bound. Recommend leaving as-is; the post's range is the more honest reading of the $8-12B cost range divided by $2B.

## CORRECTION 4 (MINOR -- consider clarifying)

**Whirlwind construction start: post says 1947; some sources say 1948.**

Current text (paragraph beginning "Construction began in 1947..."):

> Construction began in 1947 in a brick building on the corner of Massachusetts Avenue and Vassar Street -- the **Barta Building**, MIT designation N42.

Two Wikipedia articles consulted ("Whirlwind I" and "Project Whirlwind") both give 1948 as the construction start, while the post says 1947. Redmond and Smith's "Project Whirlwind: The History of a Pioneer Computer" (1980), the canonical scholarly history, would be the authoritative source. The 1947 date may reflect detailed design start while the 1948 date reflects assembly start. This is a hairline distinction. Recommend: leave as-is; the Redmond and Smith book is the post's cited source.
