# DRAFT CORRECTIONS — Post 11: The Magician Who Told No Secrets

Fact-verification 2026-05-17. Post is in very good shape overall. Only four small precision issues are worth touching; none change the narrative.

## Verdict

The post is factually accurate on all major points. The Beurling biographical details (1905 Gothenburg birth, 1986 Princeton death, 1933 Uppsala PhD, 1937 Uppsala professorship, 1948-49 Harvard visit, 1954 IAS appointment), the Geheimschreiber details (10 wheels, "Sturgeon" codename, two weeks pen-and-paper, 25 May 1940 first traffic, 500K/350K messages, FRA founding 1 July 1942), the Polish codebreakers section (Rejewski 27 from Bydgoszcz, December 1932 Enigma break, Pyry July 1939), and all the major quotes (Ahlfors "best friend", Kahn "finest feat", Bauer "magnificent achievements", Beckman "every bit as impressive") all check out against primary or near-primary sources.

Four small precision issues recommended.

## Correction 1: Karin Beurling — "distinguished biochemist" overstates the source

**Post (line 133):**
> Karin Beurling, Arne's second wife -- herself a distinguished biochemist -- prepared meals during these marathon sessions.

**Issue:** Wikipedia describes Karin as a "distinguished Ph.D. student from Uppsala University" who "worked in a biochemistry lab at Princeton University." Her field of doctoral study is not stated as biochemistry. "Distinguished biochemist" is not what the sources support.

**Suggested edit:**
> Karin Beurling, Arne's second wife -- herself a Uppsala PhD who worked in a biochemistry lab at Princeton -- prepared meals during these marathon sessions.

Alternative milder edit (keeps cadence):
> Karin Beurling, Arne's second wife -- herself an accomplished scientist who worked in a biochemistry lab at Princeton -- prepared meals during these marathon sessions.

## Correction 2: T52 timeline — "T52d/T52e from 1942" mixes two upgrades

**Post (line 55):**
> Later T52d/T52e variants (introduced from 1942) added irregular wheel stepping that pushed the keyspace to roughly 10^27 -- and Beurling never tackled those; Bletchley Park failed to break them either.

**Issue:** The chronology compresses three different upgrades. The Germans rolled out T52c ("Caesar") on 21 July 1942 — directly in response to the Stewen leak. T52d came in 1943 (Bletchley first broke it in June 1944, "Halibut"). T52e debuted only in late 1944 (and was never broken). The post jumps to T52d/e and skips T52c entirely.

**Suggested edit:**
> The Germans responded with a series of upgrades -- T52c in mid-1942, T52d in 1943, T52e in late 1944 -- adding irregular wheel stepping that eventually pushed the keyspace to roughly 10^27. The Swedes broke versions through the mid-1943 upgrade and lost the traffic after that. Beurling never tackled the later variants. Bletchley Park first cracked T52d only in June 1944 ("Halibut," via a depth of five), and never broke T52e at all.

If the author prefers brevity:
> Later T52c/d/e variants (rolled out 1942-1944) added irregular wheel stepping that pushed the keyspace to roughly 10^27. Beurling never tackled those, and Bletchley Park first broke T52d only in June 1944 -- never T52e.

## Correction 3: "By late 1943" — the window actually closed earlier

**Post (line 109):**
> By late 1943, the Germans had changed their methods and the window closed.

**Issue:** The leak via Colonel Stewen reached the Germans around 17 June 1942. T52c ("Caesar") appeared in traffic on 21 July 1942. A second German upgrade in mid-1943 was the one the Swedes could not defeat (per Wikipedia/Rutherford Journal). The window thus closed across 1942-1943, not specifically "by late 1943." This is a minor tightening.

**Suggested edit:**
> By mid-1943, the Germans had upgraded the machine beyond Swedish reach and the window closed.

Or:
> Across 1942 and into 1943, the Germans rolled out upgrades the Swedes could not defeat, and the window closed.

## Correction 4 (optional, only if rigorous): "Room 115"

**Post (lines 129, 222):**
> he moved into **Albert Einstein's former office -- Room 115**.
> ... Died in Einstein's office. Room 115.

**Issue:** Wikipedia and Acta Mathematica only say Beurling "took over Einstein's office" — no room number. The "Room 115" detail likely traces to Beckman 2002 or Princeton lore. **If Beckman 2002 explicitly says Room 115, the post is fine; if not, recommend dropping the room number.** This is the one factual claim in the post not verified by primary or near-primary online sources.

**Recommendation:** Verify against Beckman 2002 (in the author's possession per footnote 4). If Beckman says Room 115, keep. If not, drop the number:

> he moved into **Albert Einstein's former office**.
> ...
> Died in Einstein's office. Still not explaining how he did it.

## Non-corrections (worth noting but no change recommended)

- **"Arne Carl-August Beurling"** — Wikipedia uses "Carl-August," Acta Math memorial uses "Karl-August." Both Swedish spellings are valid. Post follows Wikipedia. Leave as is.
- **Bauer quote truncation** — Bauer actually wrote "one of the most magnificent achievements of cryptanalysis **of that time**." Post drops "of that time" — fine in a popular-history piece.
- **Beurling-Malliavin theorem 1960-61** — Acta lists Beurling-Malliavin papers in 1962 and 1967. The "1960-61 working period" fits the first paper's publication lead-time. Verified consistent.
- **"first electromechanical cryptanalytic machine in history" (bomba)** — sometimes contested (some accounts call the Polish "cyclometer" from late 1934 the first cryptanalytic machine, not bomba), but bomba was certainly the first dedicated to a specific cipher-breaking operation. Leave as is.
