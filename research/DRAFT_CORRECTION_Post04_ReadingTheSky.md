## Errors and Required Revisions

### 1. Attribution: who "developed" the conveyor belt model

**Current text (line 37):**
> "In the 1970s and 1980s, **Keith Browning** at the Met Office revolutionized how we read cyclones by developing the **conveyor belt model** (Browning, 1986)."

**Problem:** The conveyor belt terminology was introduced by **Harrold (1973)** and **Carlson (1980)**, both cited in the modern literature as the origin. **Young et al. (1987)** also modified the model. Browning fostered, synthesized, and popularized the concept through a series of publications including 1985 (with Hill, on the polar trough conveyor belt) and his landmark 1986 review. Browning did not solely "develop" the model.

**Suggested revision:**
> "In the 1970s and 1980s, **Keith Browning** at the Met Office championed and synthesized the **conveyor belt model** of cyclones (Browning, 1986) — a framework whose key elements had been introduced by Harrold (1973) and Carlson (1980)..."

This also fits better with the existing Harrold (1973) citation in the next paragraph, which is currently parenthetical and undermotivated. (Better: explicitly note in the body that the WCB concept dates to Harrold 1973.)

### 2. "Sometimes rising through 600 hPa of altitude" — wrong framing

**Current text (line 41):**
> "It originates in the low-level southwesterly flow ahead of the cold front, ascends vigorously (sometimes rising through 600 hPa of altitude), and on satellite imagery appears as the elongated cloud band above the warm front (Browning, 1986; Harrold, 1973)."

**Problems:**
- "600 hPa of altitude" conflates pressure decrease with altitude — they are not the same dimensionally.
- "Sometimes" is wrong. The modern operational definition of a WCB trajectory **is** an ascent of at least 600 hPa within 48 hours (Madonna et al. 2014; standard threshold across the literature). It's not occasional behavior; it's the defining criterion.

**Suggested revision:**
> "It originates in the low-level southwesterly flow ahead of the cold front and ascends vigorously — typically rising through 600 hPa of pressure (roughly 7-8 km of altitude) over 1-2 days. On satellite imagery it appears as the elongated cloud band above the warm front (Browning, 1986; Harrold, 1973)."

### 3. EUMeTrain quote (line 61) — subject change in pulled quote

**Current text:**
> As the **EUMeTrain** satellite manual notes: "The conveyor belt theory shows a higher variety of possible developments and clearly indicates that the Occlusion process as described by the Norwegian model only represents a special case."[^2]

**Problem:** The original sentence on the EUMeTrain page begins with **"It shows..."**, where the antecedent is conveyor belt theory itself. The post has subtly substituted "The conveyor belt theory" for "It," changing the direct quotation. Quote marks should bracket the exact text.

**Suggested revision (option A — exact quote):**
> As the **EUMeTrain** satellite manual notes: "It shows a higher variety of possible developments and clearly indicates that the Occlusion process as described by the Norwegian model only represents a special case."[^2]

**Suggested revision (option B — paraphrase, no quote marks):**
> As the **EUMeTrain** satellite manual notes, the conveyor belt theory shows a higher variety of possible developments — and clearly indicates that the occlusion process described in the Norwegian model is only a special case.[^2]

Option B is cleaner because the snipped subject substitution is a real (if minor) misquotation risk in scholarly writing.

### 4. Schultz CCB critique — slight overreach

**Current text (line 43):**
> "Its existence has been debated - David Schultz and others have questioned whether it truly represents a coherent airstream..."

**Problem:** Schultz (2001) did not deny CCB existence; he reexamined and clarified its structure, finding conflicting depictions in prior literature (cyclonic vs anticyclonic paths). The framing in the post is technically defensible but suggests skepticism stronger than what Schultz actually argued. He proposed that cyclones with well-defined warm fronts have a sharp demarcation between cyclonic and anticyclonic paths, while weaker warm fronts produce broad transition zones.

**Suggested revision:**
> "Its structure has been debated — David Schultz (2001) reexamined the CCB and showed that what appears as one airstream in some depictions is really two distinct paths (cyclonic and anticyclonic) — but the concept remains useful for understanding low-level flow north of warm fronts."

## Minor Notes (no action required, but worth flagging)

- **Cold front wind shift SW->NW** (line 26) is a simplification. Real-world: SW before passage, then through W to NW after. The post is correct in net direction but skips the southeast pre-passage Wikipedia mentions for an idealized case. Probably fine as written for a general-audience post.
- **Warm front pressure tendency "levels off after passage"** (line 27) — slight simplification of Wikipedia "slight rise followed by a decrease." Probably fine.
- **SEVIRI water vapor channel "6-7 micrometer"** — SEVIRI has two WV channels at 6.25 µm and 7.35 µm. The "6-7 micrometer" descriptor is loose but defensible (it covers the lower channel's range and is colloquially common). Probably fine for general audience.
- **Browning at "the Met Office"** — true; Wikipedia confirms Met Office among his affiliations. He also worked at Air Force Cambridge Research Labs and University of Reading.
