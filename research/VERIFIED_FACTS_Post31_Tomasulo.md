## Verified Facts

### Robert Tomasulo
- **Born 31 October 1934** (Wikipedia says 31 Oct 1934, NOT 25 January 1934 as the brief stated)
- **Died 3 April 2008** (NOT "March 2008" as the brief stated)
- Manhattan College -> Syracuse University engineering degree (BS, then engineering degree, not confirmed if MS)
- Joined IBM 1956; 25-year IBM career
- 1997 Eckert-Mauchly Award - citation "For the ingenious Tomasulo algorithm, which enabled out-of-order execution processors to be implemented" CONFIRMED
- Post says he attended Regis High School - confirmed
- IBM Journal of R&D 11(1):25-33, January 1967, "An Efficient Algorithm for Exploiting Multiple Arithmetic Units" CONFIRMED

### Lynn Conway
- Born 2 January 1938, Mount Vernon NY CONFIRMED
- Died 9 June 2024, Jackson MI CONFIRMED
- Joined IBM Research Yorktown 1964 CONFIRMED
- Fired by IBM 1968 CONFIRMED
- IBM apology 14 October 2020 by Diane Gherson at virtual event "Tech Trailblazer and Transgender Pioneer" CONFIRMED (1200 IBM employees)
- 23 February 1966 DIS memo: Conway/Randell/Rozenberg/Senzig four authors - confirmed by post's URL (PDF at ai.eecs.umich.edu)

### Pentium Pro
- Launched 1 November 1995 CONFIRMED
- $974 starting price (150 MHz model) CONFIRMED via Wikipedia secondary, Day in Tech History
- 5.5 million transistors CONFIRMED
- **PROCESS DISCREPANCY**: Wikipedia says 150 MHz used 0.50 micron BiCMOS (NOT 0.6 micron). Post says: "The launch part ran at 150 megahertz on a 0.6 micron BiCMOS process". The 0.6 micron process was the 133 MHz prototype/initial version. The 150 MHz and 166 MHz versions were 0.35/0.50 micron BiCMOS. This may need correction or qualification.

### Pentium Pro process (NUANCED)
- ISSCC paper Feb 1995 by Colwell & Steck: "A 150 MHz 0.6 µm BiCMOS Superscalar Microprocessor" (the prototype/research die) - this is what Colwell published
- Actual launched 150 MHz part on 1 November 1995: **0.50 micron BiCMOS** per multiple primary sources (Wikipedia Pentium Pro, MPR Nov 1995)
- 166/180/200 MHz parts: 0.35 micron BiCMOS
- The post's claim "The launch part ran at 150 megahertz on a 0.6 micron BiCMOS process" is INACCURATE for the launched product; it conflates the ISSCC research-paper process with the production-launch process.
- Possible fix: change to "0.50 micron BiCMOS" or hedge as "early production parts moved from 0.6 to 0.5 micron BiCMOS by launch"
