## REQUIRED CORRECTION

### El Chichon eruption dates

**Location:** Section "1982-83: the surprise that triggered TOGA", paragraph beginning "Second, the satellite-based sea-surface-temperature retrievals..."

**Current text:**
> El Chichon, a volcano in the southern Mexican state of Chiapas, had erupted on 28 March and again on 4 April 1982.

**Problem:** The dates are wrong. There were THREE plinian eruptions, on 29 March, 3 April, and 4 April 1982. The post drops 3 April entirely and gives 28 March instead of 29 March.

**Source for correct dates:** Wikipedia "El Chichón" article ("three plinian eruptions (March 29, April 3, and April 4)"); Carey & Sigurdsson 1986; standard volcanological literature.

**Proposed replacement text:**
> El Chichon, a volcano in the southern Mexican state of Chiapas, had erupted in a sequence of three plinian eruptions on 29 March, 3 April, and 4 April 1982.

### Cane-Zebiak 1996-97 forecast characterization

**Location:** Section "1996-1997: the array sees a warm wave coming", paragraph on Cane-Zebiak performance.

**Current text:**
> The model's January 1997 forecast called for "continuation of somewhat below normal Nino 3 conditions, with strongest negative anomalies for winter 1996-97" -- **weak La Nina conditions**, exactly the opposite of what was about to happen.

**Issue:** The quoted phrase actually comes from the **March 1996** CPC bulletin (Zebiak & Cane), not a January 1997 forecast. The Cane-Zebiak forecasts run during 1996 and into 1997 did consistently underpredict the developing event, but the specific quoted text is from March 1996. The March 1997 bulletin actually said the model predicted "below normal Niño 3 conditions for spring 1997, returning to normal by winter 1997-98 and becoming slightly warm in early 1998" - which is not pure La Nina either.

**Proposed clarification:**
> The model's forecasts run during 1996, including the March 1996 *Experimental Long-Lead Forecast Bulletin*, called for "continuation of somewhat below normal Nino 3 conditions, with strongest negative anomalies for winter 1996-97" -- weak La Nina conditions through the year ahead. By March 1997 the forecast had softened: the model was now predicting cool conditions through spring 1997, returning to neutral by winter 1997-98, and only "slightly warm" early in 1998. The actual event by December 1997 was a peak Nino 3.4 anomaly of +2.5 degrees C - one of the largest El Ninos on record.

This is a meaningful refinement because the post's current attribution of the quote to "January 1997" is a sourcing error - the quoted phrase came from the March 1996 CPC bulletin. The broader story (Cane-Zebiak underpredicted the 1997-98 event at long lead) is correct.
