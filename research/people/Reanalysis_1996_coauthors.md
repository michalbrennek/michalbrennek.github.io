# Kalnay et al. 1996 BAMS — Co-author Sketches

Full citation: Kalnay, E., M. Kanamitsu, R. Kistler, W. Collins, D. Deaven, L. Gandin, M. Iredell, S. Saha, G. White, J. Woollen, Y. Zhu, M. Chelliah, W. Ebisuzaki, W. Higgins, J. Janowiak, K. C. Mo, C. Ropelewski, J. Wang, A. Leetmaa, R. Reynolds, Roy Jenne, Dennis Joseph, 1996: "The NCEP/NCAR 40-Year Reanalysis Project", BAMS 77(3): 437-471 (March 1996).

The paper has, by citation count, become one of the most-cited papers in all the geosciences. At the time of Kanamitsu's 2011 death the paper had been cited 7,985 times; the current count is well into five figures.

The 22-author list divides cleanly into three institutional blocks plus two "outside contributor" names appended at the end:

1. **NMC/NCEP modeling and analysis core (first ~12 names)** — E. Kalnay (director), M. Kanamitsu, R. Kistler, W. Collins, D. Deaven, L. Gandin, M. Iredell, S. Saha, G. White, J. Woollen, Y. Zhu — the operational EMC team that built and ran the system.

2. **NCEP/CPC diagnostics group (the next ~6 names)** — M. Chelliah, W. Ebisuzaki, W. Higgins, J. Janowiak, K. C. Mo, C. Ropelewski, J. Wang — the Climate Prediction Center side, validating and characterising the output.

3. **Ocean and surface-data extension** — A. Leetmaa (then heading the NCEP Coupled Model Project / shortly to lead the Climate Prediction Center), R. Reynolds (SST analysis).

4. **NCAR side** — Roy Jenne and Dennis Joseph, the NCAR data-archive scientists who supplied the historical observational record.

This is the institutional logic of the byline: by 1996, the NCEP/NCAR Reanalysis was no longer a single-team project but a multi-institution operation, and the author order encodes the division of labour.

## 1. Eugenia Kalnay (1942-2024)

See companion sketch [Eugenia_Kalnay.md](Eugenia_Kalnay.md). At the time of the paper she was Director of EMC at NCEP, the principal scientific lead of the reanalysis project, and the paper's corresponding author.

## 2. Masao Kanamitsu (1943-2011)

Japanese-American atmospheric scientist; **born 6 November 1943, Kumamoto, Japan; died 17 August 2011, Del Mar, California, of cancer, aged 67**. At the time of the 1996 paper he was Branch Chief of the NCEP Global Modeling Branch, where he led the implementation of advances in the NCEP global spectral model that substantially improved operational forecast skill. He was the senior modeling lead under Kalnay, and the natural second author. His work specifically on the reanalysis won him a Group Gold Medal from the Department of Commerce in 1997.

In 2001 he moved from NCEP to the **Scripps Institution of Oceanography** at UC San Diego, where he worked for the rest of his life. At Scripps he was a major figure in regional spectral modeling and seasonal prediction. The 2014 BAMS "Regional Spectral Model Workshop in Memory of John Roads and Masao Kanamitsu" is the canonical Scripps tribute. He was also the lead developer of the NCEP/DOE Reanalysis-2 (R2), the 1998 follow-on intended to correct known errors in R1.

The Wikipedia article on Kanamitsu and the Scripps obituary are the best sources.

## 3. Robert Kistler (NCEP)

Veteran NCEP analysis scientist; co-author on the original 1996 BAMS paper and lead author on the 2001 BAMS follow-up paper "The NCEP-NCAR 50-Year Reanalysis: Monthly Means CD-ROM and Documentation". Worked at NCEP through the 1990s and 2000s on the operational reanalysis machinery, particularly the data-assimilation pipeline and the production of the gridded output files. Detailed biographical info not retrieved this session.

## 4. William Collins (NCEP)

NCEP analysis scientist; worked on the observational data-stream side of the reanalysis — quality control of radiosonde, aircraft, and surface observations. Not to be confused with William D. Collins (NCAR/LBNL atmospheric chemist, lead of CAM atmospheric model in the 2000s) — different person. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 5. Dick Deaven (NCEP)

NCEP analysis scientist; worked on observational data ingest and SSI (Spectral Statistical Interpolation, the OI-successor used at NCEP). Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 6. Lev Gandin (1923?-1997)

See full sketch in [Lev_Gandin.md](Lev_Gandin.md). Émigré pioneer of OI; born Leningrad early 1920s, worked at the Main Geophysical Observatory through 1981, emigrated to the US c.1991 and joined NMC as a UCAR Scientist. Co-author on Kalnay et al. 1996. He brought the Complex Quality Control (CQC) framework — developed at MGO and published in his 1988 MWR paper — to the reanalysis, where it was applied retrospectively to the entire 1957-1996 observational record. **He died in 1997, a year after the BAMS paper was published.** The poignant detail for Post 46: Gandin co-authored the paper that consolidated, on a 40-year timescale, the technique he had invented thirty-three years earlier in Leningrad. Cross-link to Post 44 (OI/Gandin).

## 7. Mark Iredell (NCEP)

NCEP modeler; long-time lead of the NCEP Global Forecast System (GFS) post-processing and the GRIB/GRIB2 data format work. At the time of the 1996 paper he was a Global Modeling Branch scientist working on the spectral model. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 8. Suranjana Saha (NCEP)

Indian-American meteorologist at NCEP/EMC; affiliated with the NOAA Center for Weather and Climate Prediction in College Park, Maryland. In the 1996 paper she was a junior author on the operational analysis side; she went on to become the **lead author of the Climate Forecast System Reanalysis (CFSR, 2010 BAMS)** and of the CFSv2 paper (2014 Journal of Climate, operational at NCEP from March 2011). The reanalysis baton passed from Kanamitsu/Kalnay (R1, 1996) and Kanamitsu (R2, 2002) to Saha (CFSR, 2010); she is, with Kalnay and Kanamitsu, one of the three operational leads of the NCEP reanalysis tradition. Her CFSR paper is itself one of the most-cited papers in atmospheric science.

## 9. Glenn H. White (NCEP)

NCEP diagnostics scientist; long-time evaluator of operational forecast skill at NCEP and lead of the operational forecast-skill scorecards. Worked closely with Kalnay through the 1990s on the EMC operational suite. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 10. John Woollen (NCEP)

NCEP analysis scientist; one of the lead developers of NCEP's data assimilation system through the 1990s, including the conversion from Spectral Statistical Interpolation (SSI) to the Gridpoint Statistical Interpolation (GSI). His name appears on essentially all of NCEP's data-assimilation papers from the 1990s through the 2010s. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 11. Yong Zhu (NCEP)

NCEP scientist on the operational analysis side; later worked on satellite data assimilation. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 12. Muthuvel Chelliah (NCEP/CPC)

NCEP Climate Prediction Center scientist; specialist in atmospheric circulation diagnostics, particularly stratospheric circulation and the QBO. Long career at CPC; co-author on many of the 1990s-2000s reanalysis diagnostic papers. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 13. Wesley Ebisuzaki (NCEP/CPC)

NCEP Climate Prediction Center meteorologist; **at CPC since 1989**. In the Development Branch. Major contributor to the post-publication evaluation of the reanalysis — known biases, trends, and the development of the CORe (Conventional Observation Reanalysis) system for climate monitoring. Co-author with Kanamitsu on the R2 (NCEP/DOE Reanalysis-2) overview paper. Still active at NCEP/CPC.

## 14. Wayne Higgins (NCEP/CPC, later NOAA CPO Director)

US climate scientist; **PhD and MS in Meteorology from Penn State, BS in Physics from University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign**. At the time of the 1996 paper he was a CPC scientist working on the diagnostic side. He went on to:
- **Director, Climate Prediction Center (CPC), July 2007 - 2013**
- **Acting Director of NCEP, 2013**
- **Director, NOAA Climate Program Office (CPO), July 2013 - December 2023**

So Higgins is the only one of the 1996 co-authors who ascended to become NOAA's chief climate executive. He conceived and led the 2004 North American Monsoon Experiment (NAME). Publications: 80+ peer-reviewed papers.

## 15. John Janowiak (NCEP/CPC)

CPC scientist; specialist in global precipitation analysis — long-time lead of the CMAP (CPC Merged Analysis of Precipitation) dataset and the GPCP. Later moved to University of Maryland (AOSC). Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 16. Kingtse C. Mo (NCEP/CPC)

NCEP Climate Prediction Center scientist; specialist in climate variability, drought, and the North American climate diagnostics. Detailed biography not retrieved.

## 17. Chester F. "Chet" Ropelewski (NCEP/CPC, then IRI Columbia)

US climate scientist; **fifty years of experience in climate analysis**. At the time of the 1996 paper he was **Chief of the Analysis Branch of the Climate Prediction Center at NOAA**. He is most famous for the **Ropelewski and Halpert 1986/1987 MWR papers** that produced the first robust global analysis of ENSO regional precipitation and temperature impacts — the foundational empirical work of modern ENSO teleconnections, cross-link to Post 27 (Cane/Zebiak).

Awards: **Norbert Gerbier-Mumm Award (WMO) 1990; AMS Fellow 2001**. Author of 100+ papers. After NOAA he became **Director of the Climate Monitoring section at the International Research Institute for Climate and Society (IRI) at Columbia University**, and Senior Policy Analyst and Advisor at The Earth Institute. Co-author of the 2011 Cambridge textbook *Climate Analysis* (with Phillip A. Arkin).

## 18. Jia Wang (NCEP)

NCEP scientist; on the diagnostics side. Detailed biography not retrieved. (Caveat: "Jia Wang" is a common name — there are at least two atmospheric/ocean scientists by this name. The 1996 paper's Jia Wang appears to be the NCEP CPC scientist working on diagnostic monitoring.)

## 19. Ants Leetmaa (NCEP, then GFDL Director)

**Estonian-American oceanographer and climate scientist**. **PhD in Oceanography from MIT** (research assistant there until 1972). Career path:
- 1972: NOAA Atlantic Oceanographic and Meteorological Laboratories, Miami — Chief Scientist on research cruises, ~550 days at sea including the 1982-83 El Niño
- 1986: moved to **NMC, Washington DC**
- 1991: Chief of the NCEP Coupled Model Project, leading development of the coupled ocean-atmosphere model that became the basis for NCEP's seasonal/interannual predictions
- (at time of 1996 paper:) heading the coupled model effort; about to become Director of CPC
- **1997-2001: Director, NOAA NWS Climate Prediction Center**
- **2001-2007: Director, GFDL** — the third director in GFDL's history, after Joseph Smagorinsky and Jerry Mahlman (cross-link to Post 16 GFDL)
- Retired with the "Ants Leetmaa Symposium" at GFDL in his honour

He is the single most institutionally consequential of the 1996 co-authors after Kalnay herself: he went on to lead two of the three big NOAA modeling labs.

## 20. Richard W. Reynolds (NCEP)

NCEP scientist; the canonical authority on operational sea surface temperature analysis. **The "Reynolds OISST" (Optimum Interpolation SST) products from the early 1990s onward** are the operational SST product used as boundary conditions for the reanalysis and many other applications. His 1994 J. Climate paper "Improved global sea surface temperature analyses using optimum interpolation" is one of the most-cited SST analysis papers. Co-author on the Reynolds et al. 2002 J. Climate "An Improved In Situ and Satellite SST Analysis for Climate" and the Reynolds et al. 2007 J. Climate "Daily High-Resolution-Blended Analyses for Sea Surface Temperature". Long career at NCEP, later moved to NCDC (NCEI). Detailed biographical info beyond this professional record not retrieved.

## 21. Roy L. Jenne (1931-2016)

**NCAR's data-archive legend**. **Born 1931, died 2016**. For decades the head of NCAR's Data Support Section (later the Research Data Archive). Under his direction, NCAR became one of the world's primary archives of observational and model data for weather and climate research, including the global atmospheric reanalyses and the IPCC. 

For the NCEP/NCAR Reanalysis specifically, Jenne was the indispensable other half of the project: NCEP had the model and the operational data assimilation system; NCAR (through Jenne's group) had the historical observations — the recovered land surface, ship, rawinsonde, pibal, aircraft, satellite and other data going back to 1948. Without Jenne's decades of data-rescue work — much of it physical: punched-card decks, paper logbooks, microfiche — there would have been no observational stream to assimilate, and no reanalysis. He is, in the historical narrative, the unsung hero of the project.

A memorial gathering was held at NCAR's Mesa Lab Damon Room on **16 February 2017** (3-5 pm). The NCAR Research Data Archive blog tribute "Remembering Roy Jenne (1931-2016)" is the canonical obituary.

This is the single most narratively important "non-NCEP" co-author for the post: Jenne represents the data-rescue side of NWP, the patient archival work that made reanalysis possible, and his 2016 death (twenty years after the BAMS paper) gives a natural narrative closing-arc moment.

## 22. Dennis Joseph (NCAR)

NCAR Data Support Section scientist; Roy Jenne's longtime colleague. The "Joseph and Jenne" partnership on the NCAR side of the reanalysis is comparable to the Kalnay/Kanamitsu partnership on the NCEP side. Detailed biographical info not retrieved.
