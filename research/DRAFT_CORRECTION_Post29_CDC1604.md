## SERIOUS ERRORS (must be fixed before publication)

### 1. CDC-IBM antitrust settlement amount

**Post says (paragraph "Coda"):** "It survived a major IBM antitrust settlement in 1973 (CDC received six hundred million dollars)..."

**Correct:** The 1973 IBM-CDC antitrust settlement was approximately **$80 million** in cash and assets, including the transfer of IBM's Service Bureau Corporation. Multiple sources (Wikipedia "Control Data Corporation", TIME magazine archive of "Settlement for IBM", and IBM antitrust documents) confirm the $80 million figure.

The post's $600 million figure may be confusing this with the cumulative damages CDC sought, or with later IBM antitrust matters with the federal government, but the actual settlement received by CDC was about $80 million. **This is a substantial error of magnitude (~7.5x).**

**Action:** Change "six hundred million dollars" to "approximately eighty million dollars in cash and assets, including the transfer of IBM's Service Bureau Corporation."

### 2. CDC 1604 announcement venue: Western Joint Computer Conference, Los Angeles

**Post says ("Building the 1604" section):** "CDC publicly announced the machine on **16 October 1959** at the Western Joint Computer Conference in Los Angeles."

**Correct:** The CDC 1604 release date of October 16, 1959 is correct. But the **WJCC 1959 was in San Francisco on March 3-5, 1959** — not in October, not in Los Angeles. The **Eastern Joint Computer Conference 1959** was in Boston, December 1-3, 1959.

No JCC was held in October 1959. The October 16 1959 announcement was most likely a corporate press release, not at a major industry conference. (Joint Computer Conference - Wikipedia.)

**Action:** Remove the WJCC / Los Angeles attribution. Replace with "publicly announced the machine on 16 October 1959" without the venue claim, or verify a specific announcement venue from CDC corporate sources.

### 3. Cray's father was a civil engineer, not a city engineer

**Post says (Cray bio paragraph):** "He had been born on **28 September 1925** in Chippewa Falls, a small lumber town in northwestern Wisconsin where his father was the city engineer."

**Correct:** Per the Wikipedia article on Seymour Cray, his father was a **civil engineer** (a profession), not a "city engineer" (a municipal job title). This is a factual misstatement. Some sources say his father was the Chippewa Falls city engineer, but Wikipedia explicitly distinguishes — Markoff's NYT obit doesn't use the "city engineer" framing either. The terms are not interchangeable.

**Action:** Change "city engineer" to "civil engineer" unless a primary source can be cited supporting the city engineer claim.

### 4. FNWF was renamed from NANWEP in 1961, not founded in 1959

**Post says (paragraph 2):** "Eight months later, in August 1960, the Naval Postgraduate School's Fleet Numerical Weather Facility (FNWF), running on the same CDC 1604..."

**More accurate:** In August 1960, the unit was still called **NANWEP** (Navy Numerical Weather Problems group), which had moved to NPS Monterey in March 1959 under Captain Wolff. It was renamed Fleet Numerical Weather Facility (FNWF) only in **1961**.

The post elsewhere ("Spanagel Hall, January 1960" paragraph) calls it "FNWF" throughout — this is anachronistic for the January-August 1960 period.

**Action:** Either (a) introduce the NANWEP-then-FNWF naming history when first mentioning the facility, or (b) refer to it as "Wolff's group" or "the Navy numerical weather group" for the 1959-1960 period.

### 5. Cray Research startup capital: $250,000 vs $300,000

**Post says (Coda):** "He founded **Cray Research, Inc.** with three hundred thousand dollars of CDC's seed money..."

**Disputed:** Wikipedia "Seymour Cray" gives the figure as $250,000 of CDC start-up money from Norris. Other secondary sources give $300,000. The post's figure may be correct but is not the dominant figure in the most authoritative source (Wikipedia citing the Murray book).

**Action:** Either cite $250,000 from Murray/Wikipedia, or footnote the $300,000 with the specific source claim.

### 6. Cray-3 / Graywolf treatment

**Post says (Coda):** "Cray Research shipped... and the cancelled Cray-3 (Graywolf) in the early 1990s."

**Correction:** The Cray-3 was NOT a Cray Research product — it was a **Cray Computer Corporation** product (CCC was Cray's separate post-1989 company). Graywolf was the specific Cray-3 prototype delivered to NCAR on May 24, 1993 as a loaner/evaluation unit (one unit; never paid for; CCC went bankrupt in March 1995). The Cray-3 was not "cancelled" — one unit shipped. The Cray-4 was the cancelled follow-on design.

The post conflates the Cray-3 program with the Cray-4 cancellation, and incorrectly attributes the Cray-3 to Cray Research (it was CCC).

**Action:** Rewrite to: "Cray Research shipped the Cray-1 in 1976 and the Cray-2 in 1985. Cray himself left Cray Research in 1989 over a strategic disagreement on multi-processor design and founded a third company, **Cray Computer Corporation**, in Colorado Springs, which delivered a single Cray-3 prototype (named Graywolf) to NCAR in May 1993 and went bankrupt in 1995 before completing the Cray-4."

### 7. "Westinghouse-trained electrical engineer" for Norris

He later worked for Westinghouse in Chicago selling X-Ray equipment (not as an engineer trainee in the standard sense). "Westinghouse-trained" overstates the Westinghouse role and undercuts the Nebraska training. Also, Norris was born near **Red Cloud, Nebraska**, and attended a tiny school in Inavale — describing him as "from Inavale" is technically fine, but combine this with "Westinghouse-trained" is misleading.

### 9. Excerpt and intro "the first fully transistorised scientific computer"

**Post says (excerpt):** "It was the first fully transistorised scientific computer."

**More precise:** Per Wikipedia, the CDC 1604 was "one of the first commercially successful transistorized computers" — not necessarily "the first" fully-transistorized. The IBM 7090 was also transistorized, announced earlier (November 1958), and other transistor scientific computers existed (e.g., Philco TRANSAC S-2000). The 1604 was probably the first commercial transistor scientific computer designed from the ground up by a startup, but the bold "first fully transistorised scientific computer" claim is overstated.

**Action:** Soften to "one of the first fully transistorised commercial scientific computers" or "the first transistor scientific computer designed by Control Data Corporation."

### 14. "Contemporary tube-era IBM 709" — comparison muddled

**Post says ("Building the 1604" section):** "The total power draw of the production 1604 was about **5.5 kilowatts**, against roughly 50 to 60 kilowatts for the contemporary tube-era IBM 709."

**Imprecise:** The IBM **709** was indeed tube-era (released August 1958). But the actual **contemporary** machine of the CDC 1604 was the transistorized **IBM 7090** (announced 1958, first installation December 1959 — basically contemporaneous with the 1604). The 7090 had reduced power requirements (~70% reduction vs tube machines). Comparing 1604 power to the older 709 rather than the contemporary 7090 understates how competitive the IBM transistor machine was on power. The "contemporary tube-era IBM 709" phrase is internally contradictory — 709 was tube-era and not contemporary; 7090 was contemporary and not tube-era.

**Action:** Reframe the comparison: either (a) "against the tube-era IBM 709 (around 50-60 kW)" without "contemporary," or (b) compare to the IBM 7090's specs directly.

### 15. Date precision: Cray accident date

**Post says (Coda):** "On **22 or 23 September 1996** -- sources differ by a day -- Cray was driving his Jeep Cherokee on Interstate 25..."

**More accurate:** The accident occurred on **Sunday, September 22, 1996**, per UPI archives. The September 23 figure may come from sources that reported the next day. There's no real "sources differ by a day" issue — the accident was Sunday Sept 22 and was reported by media on Monday Sept 23.

**Action:** Change to "On Sunday, **22 September 1996**, Cray was driving his Jeep Cherokee..." Remove the "or 23" / "sources differ by a day" framing as misleading.
