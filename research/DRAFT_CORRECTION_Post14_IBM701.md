## REQUIRED CORRECTIONS

### Correction 1: ENIAC unveiling date (line 36)

**Current text:**
> When the ENIAC was unveiled in 1944, Watson Sr. was furious.

**Issue:** ENIAC was publicly unveiled on February 14-15, 1946. Construction began in June 1944, but the machine was secret until early 1946. Watson Sr.'s reaction to ENIAC was after its 1946 public unveiling, not 1944.

**Suggested replacement:**
> When the ENIAC was unveiled in 1946, Watson Sr. was furious.

This also strengthens the timeline because the SSEC's accelerated construction (August 1947 - January 1948) makes much more sense as a response to the February 1946 ENIAC unveiling than to a hypothetical 1944 reveal.

### Correction 2: Watson Jr. education (line 40)

**Current text:**
> But Watson Jr. -- born 1914, MIT-educated, a wartime bomber pilot -- saw what his father could not.

**Issue:** Watson Jr. attended **Brown University**, not MIT. He graduated from Brown in 1937 with a business degree. The post's own image caption later ("Brown University photo, 1937") directly contradicts the body text.

**Suggested replacement:**
> But Watson Jr. -- born 1914, Brown-educated, a wartime pilot who flew Lend-Lease missions to Moscow -- saw what his father could not.

(The "bomber pilot" element can be retained but it understates: he was personal pilot to General Follett Bradley, head of Lend-Lease to the USSR, and flew the route to Moscow. He reached Lt. Col.)

### Correction 3: Hutchins quote attribution (line 222)

**Current text:**
> It was, as the historian **John Hutchins** later put it, "a clever proof-of-concept engineered to make the 701 look miraculous to reporters."[^8]

**Issue:** This is not a direct verbatim quote from Hutchins 2004. Hutchins describes the experiment's small scale and notes the rules were "fitted to specific words and sentences" but does not use this exact phrasing. The post presents a paraphrase as a quotation with quotation marks.

**Suggested replacement (option A - drop the quotation marks):**
> It was, as the historian **John Hutchins** later argued, a clever proof-of-concept engineered to make the 701 look miraculous to reporters.[^8]

**Suggested replacement (option B - use actual Hutchins phrasing):**
> The historian **John Hutchins** later concluded that the experiment was small-scale -- just 250 words and six grammar rules -- with many operations fitted to specific words and sentences for the demonstration.[^8]
