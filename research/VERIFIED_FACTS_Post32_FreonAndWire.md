## CONFIRMED FACTS

1. CDC 7600 announced 3 December 1968 (Computer History Museum: "Control Data Corporation announces its 7600 model").
2. CDC 7600 SN 12 delivered to NCAR May 1971, decommissioned 1 April 1983 (NCAR/CISL: "took delivery of CDC 7600 - serial number 12 - in May 1971"; "decommissioned and replaced in the spring of 1983 by a second Cray-1A - serial number 14").
3. CDC 7600 clock: 27.5 ns (Wikipedia: "Clock speed 36.4 MHz (27.5 ns cycle)"). NCAR/CISL says "clock speed of 27 nanoseconds".
4. CDC 7600 peak: 36 MFLOPS (Wikipedia: "Peak performance: 36 MFLOPS with hand-compiled code reaching approximately 10 MFLOPS"). CHM page says "nearly 40 megaflops" - looser.
5. CDC 7600 SCM 65K words / LCM 512K words (Wikipedia and NCAR/CISL confirm).
6. CDC 7600 5x speed vs 6600 (NCAR/CISL: "generally ran at five times the speed of the CDC 6600").
7. CDC 7600 about 75 units sold (Wikipedia: "Over 75 units sold").
8. CDC 7600 physical: 188 cm tall, 95 kW @ 208V 400Hz (Wikipedia).
9. CDC 7600 C-shape: confirmed quote from HandWiki "redesigned into a large 'C' shape to allow access to the modules on either side of the cooling piping by walking into the inside of the 'C'".
10. CDC 7600 cooling by M. Dean Roush, aluminum plates + freon (HandWiki direct quote confirmed).
11. CDC 7600 downtime: "Both LLNL and NCAR reported that the machine would break down at least once a day, and often four or five times" (HandWiki confirmed).
12. CDC 8600: 4 CPUs sharing main memory, 125 MHz / 8 ns clock, ECL logic, 256 KW semiconductor memory in 64 banks (Wikipedia confirmed).
13. CDC 8600 cancelled 1974 (Wikipedia confirmed).
14. Cray cut salary to minimum wage (Wikipedia confirmed).
15. Cray-2 architecturally similar to 8600 (Wikipedia confirmed - "Cray revisited the 8600's basic design in his Cray-2").
16. STAR-100: only 3 to external customers (2 LLNL, 1 NASA Langley). Wikipedia confirms exactly: "Control Data Corporation, Arden Hills, MN (2 units); Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (2 units); NASA Langley Research Center (1 unit)" - 5 built, 3 to external customers.
17. Walter Orr Roberts founder/first director of NCAR (1960-1968) (Wikipedia confirmed).
18. NCAR founded 1960 (Wikipedia confirmed).
19. Mesa Lab architect = I.M. Pei (Wikipedia confirmed).
20. Warren Washington born 28 August 1936 in Portland Oregon (Wikipedia confirmed).

## DISCREPANCIES TO INVESTIGATE

1. **Kasahara death date**: Post says "29 March 2022" but UCAR News obituary says "March 30, 2022". Both close enough; UCAR is authoritative for institutional record. **MINOR DISCREPANCY** - 1-day difference. Possibly time-zone (Kasahara died in US Mountain Time and UCAR was reporting on a date convention).
2. **Mesa Lab ground broken**: Post says "9 June 1964". Wikipedia Mesa Laboratory says construction commenced "April 1964". Could be different events (groundbreaking ceremony vs first construction activity). **MINOR DISCREPANCY** - sources differ; post may have a more specific primary source from NCAR/UCAR archives but cannot verify within time budget.
3. **CDC seed capital**: Post acknowledges in ^33 footnote that Wikipedia says $250K vs Murray's $300K - post chose $300K. **DOCUMENTED IN POST** - not a correction.
4. **Wikipedia CDC 7600 announcement**: Wikipedia says "Released in June 1967" but Computer History Museum confirms 3 December 1968 as announcement date. Post says December 1968 (correct).
5. **CERN 7600 retirement**: CERN70 official page says "disconnected in 1984"; CERN CDS module note says "retired in 1983". Post says "to 1984" which matches CERN70 (the more authoritative institutional source) and the "twelve years" claim works.
