## ERRORS IDENTIFIED IN POST

### MAJOR ERRORS
1. **"When the ENIAC was unveiled in 1944"** -- ENIAC was unveiled February 14-15, 1946. Construction BEGAN in June 1944 but it was kept secret until 1946. This is a clear factual error.

2. **"Watson Jr. -- born 1914, MIT-educated"** -- Watson Jr. attended **Brown University**, not MIT. He graduated from Brown with a business degree in 1937. The post's own image caption (`Brown University photo, 1937`) contradicts the body text.

### MINOR ISSUES
3. The post's description of Watson Jr. as a "wartime bomber pilot" is partially accurate but understates the specificity - he was a Lt. Col. who flew General Follett Bradley (Lend-Lease) to Moscow, not a combat bomber pilot.

4. The post characterizes the Hutchins assessment as: "a clever proof-of-concept engineered to make the 701 look miraculous to reporters." This is a PARAPHRASE not a direct quote from Hutchins 2004. The post presents it as if quoted with attribution. Hutchins's actual conclusion is similar in spirit but not those words. Should be presented as paraphrase, not direct quote.

5. The post says Backus "started thinking about the problem in late 1952. In January 1953, just as the first 701s were shipping, he began a project called Speedcode." The first 701 was actually installed at IBM HQ in late December 1952 (i.e., the first 701 had already shipped). Wording is "just as the first 701s were shipping" is loose but defensible since most customer 701s shipped throughout 1953.

6. Speedcode team list: "**Donald Quarles Jr.**" -- multiple sources confirm "Donald A. Quarles, Jr." -- post is correct.

### CONFIRMED CORRECT THAT MIGHT LOOK SUSPECT
- DuPont Central Research IS confirmed as the 19th IBM 701 customer (Wikipedia full list, Wilmington DE, 1954)
- The first 701 went to IBM HQ (701 #1), Los Alamos was first CUSTOMER (701 #2) -- post handles this distinction properly
- Cressman birth year 1919 is correct
