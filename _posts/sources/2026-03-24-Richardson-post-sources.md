## 3. The Forecast Attempt

### Claims
- Used real atmospheric observations from May 20, 1910, 7:00 AM
- Attempted to compute a 6-hour forecast
- Divided atmosphere into grid of ~3,200 cells, ~200 km spacing
- Applied primitive equations of atmospheric motion (pressure, momentum, continuity)
- Used finite difference methods
- Took approximately six weeks of hand calculation
- Forecast was for a single point (near Munich)

### Sources

- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson

### Claims
- Peter Lynch at University College Dublin reanalyzed Richardson's numbers

## 11. Coastline Paradox and Fractal Connection

### Claims
- Portugal measured shared border with Spain as 987 km; Spain measured it as 1,214 km
- Richardson discovered this while researching effect of border lengths on probability of war
- Mandelbrot later connected Richardson's observations to fractal geometry

### Sources

**Wikipedia: Coastline paradox**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coastline_paradox
- Status: **Scraped successfully via curl.**
- Confirms exact text: "Shortly before 1951, Lewis Fry Richardson, in researching the possible effect of border lengths on the probability of war, noticed that the Portuguese reported their measured border with Spain to be 987 km (613 mi), but the Spanish reported it as 1,214 km (754 mi)."
- Confirms: "the first systematic study of this phenomenon was by Lewis Fry Richardson, and it was expanded upon by Benoit Mandelbrot"
- Confirms the "Richardson effect": "the sum of the segments monotonically increases when the common length of the segments decreases"

**Britannica: Coastline Paradox**
- URL: https://www.britannica.com/science/coastline-paradox
- Confirms the Richardson effect and Mandelbrot's extension
- Status: **Not scraped.**

**Medium (Simon Wrigley): "The Coastline Paradox"**
- URL: https://medium.com/swlh/the-coastline-paradox-de3adee92b65
- Popular account confirming the Portugal/Spain measurements and Richardson's role
- Status: **Not scraped.**

### Key Publication

## 13. Additional Named Facts

### "Richardson Number"
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richardson_number
- URL: https://www.britannica.com/science/Richardson-number
- A dimensionless number expressing the ratio of buoyancy to flow shear. Named after Lewis Fry Richardson. Used in turbulence studies and aviation weather forecasting.

### "Fellow of the Royal Society (1926)"
- Confirmed by: University of York page; Historic Kilmun page; Royal Society archives
- Elected FRS 1926 "for his knowledge of physics and eminence in the application of mathematics to physical problems"

### "Richardson's big whorls" quote
- *"Big whorls have little whorls, that feed on their velocity; and little whorls have lesser whorls, and so on to viscosity."*
- A parody of Jonathan Swift's verse on fleas. Appears in Richardson's *Weather Prediction by Numerical Process* (1922), p. 66.
- Widely cited; confirmed by Historic Kilmun, MacTutor, York University.

### Died one year before first operational NWP
- Richardson died September 30, 1953. The first successful operational numerical weather prediction was produced in 1954 by the Royal Swedish Air Force Weather Service, based on the work of the Joint Numerical Weather Prediction Unit (JNWPU) established in 1954 in the US.
- The ENIAC forecast (1950) was a research demonstration, not operational.
