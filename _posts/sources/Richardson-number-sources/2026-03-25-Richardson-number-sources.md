## 1. The Richardson Number - Technical

### Claims
- Dimensionless ratio of buoyancy to flow shear
- Ri < 0.25 = turbulence threshold
- First published in 1920 paper
- Used in aviation for CAT prediction (Graphical Turbulence Guidance)
- Named after Lewis Fry Richardson
- Extends Osborne Reynolds' work

### Sources

**Richardson, L. F. (1920). "The Supply of Energy from and to Atmospheric Eddies." Proc. Roy. Soc. London A, 97, 354-373.**
- DOI: 10.1098/rspa.1920.0039
- The PRIMARY source. Public domain by age.
- Could not scrape full text (403). Bibliographic details from ADS Harvard.
- Confirmed: introduces the criterion, extends Reynolds' turbulence work to gravitating atmosphere.

**Wikipedia: Richardson number**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richardson_number
- **Scraped successfully.** CC BY-SA 4.0.
- Confirms: Ri = (g/rho)(d_rho/dz)/(du/dz)^2; Ri < 0.25 necessary for turbulence (Miles 1961, Howard 1961); three variants (flux, gradient, bulk); applications in aviation, thermal convection, oceanography.

**SKYbrary Aviation Safety: Richardson Number**
- URL: https://skybrary.aero/articles/richardson-number
- **Scraped successfully.**
- Confirms: stability classification table; Ri > 0.25 = stable; 0 < Ri < 0.25 = stable but mechanical turbulence possible; Ri < 0 = unstable; component of NOAA Graphical Turbulence Guidance (GTG).

**Encyclopaedia Britannica: Richardson number**
- URL: https://www.britannica.com/science/Richardson-number
- Confirms basic definition and naming.

### Key References
- Miles, J. W. (1961). On the stability of heterogeneous shear flows. J. Fluid Mech., 10, 496-508.
- Howard, L. N. (1961). Note on a paper of John W. Miles. J. Fluid Mech., 10, 509-512.

## 2. "Big Whirls" Verse

### Claim
- Richardson paraphrased Swift's flea poem to describe turbulent cascade
- Published in 1922 book, p. 66, and in summary of 1920 paper

### Sources

**Richardson (1922), p. 66** - Public domain.
- "Big whorls have little whorls / Which feed on their velocity, / And little whorls have lesser whorls / And so on to viscosity - in the molecular sense."

**Research report confirms**: Actually a play on Augustus De Morgan's "Siphonaptera" poem, which itself reworks Jonathan Swift's 1733 "On Poetry: A Rapsody." Many sources attribute it directly to Swift; the De Morgan intermediary is more precise.

## 3. Arms Race Equations

### Claims
- dx/dt = ky - ax + g; dy/dt = lx - by + h
- Variables: x,y = arms spending; k,l = reaction; a,b = fatigue; g,h = grievance
- Stability condition: ab > kl
- Applied to WWI (1908-1914, Entente vs Central Powers)
- First written 1919, published fully 1960 posthumously
- "The equations are merely a description of what people would do if they did not stop to think" (p. 12)

### Sources (all CC BY 4.0)

**Smith, R. (2020). "The Influence of the Richardson Arms Race Model." Ch. 3 in Gleditsch (2020).**
- **Scraped successfully.** CC BY 4.0.
- Confirms: equations, variable definitions, "stop to think" quote (Richardson 1960a: 12), equilibrium analysis, WWI application, identification problem.

**Diehl, P. F. (2020). "What Richardson Got Right (and Wrong)." Ch. 4 in Gleditsch (2020).**
- **Scraped successfully.** CC BY 4.0.
- Confirms: 15-country WWI analysis, velocity vs acceleration distinction, security dilemma anticipation, counterfactual about WWI, "designed as a deterrent sometimes acted instead as an irritant" (Richardson 1960a: 55).

**Scheffran, J. (2020). "Weather, War, and Chaos." Ch. 8 in Gleditsch (2020).**
- **Scraped successfully.** CC BY 4.0.
- Confirms: eigenvalue stability analysis, 1909-14 and 1933-39 applications, Richardson's quote on stability vs equilibrium.

**Richardson (1960a). Arms and Insecurity. p. 12.**
- Posthumous. "Stop to think" quote confirmed to this page.

**Richardson (1919). The Mathematical Psychology of War. London: Hunt.**
- 50-page essay, privately printed, 35 pounds for 300 copies
- Contains Bertrand Russell's contribution (pp. 15-16)
- Written at the front in France

## 4. Statistics of Deadly Quarrels

### Claims
- Logarithmic scale: magnitude = log10(deaths)
- Mag 0 = murder, Mag 7 = world war
- Power law distribution of war sizes
- Poisson distribution of war timing
- "Counting is an antiseptic against prejudice"
- Wars are "mostly a neighborhood affair"

### Sources

**Wikipedia: Statistics of Deadly Quarrels**
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics_of_Deadly_Quarrels
- **Scraped successfully.** CC BY-SA 4.0.
- Confirms: logarithmic scale, magnitude definitions, 315 conflicts mag 2.5+, critical reception from 6 reviewers, publication history.

**sizes.com: Richardson Scale of Deadly Quarrels**
- URL: https://www.sizes.com/units/richardson_scale.htm
- **Could not scrape** (403). Content obtained by research agent.
- Confirms: magnitude examples, American Civil War logarithm illustration (3 estimates: 359,528; 279,376; 166,623 - logs 5.6, 5.4, 5.2 "bring out their substantial agreement").

**Encyclopedia.com: Lewis Fry Richardson (by Anatol Rapoport)**
- URL: https://www.encyclopedia.com/people/social-sciences-and-law/sociology-biographies/lewis-fry-richardson
- **Scraped successfully.**
- Very detailed. Confirms: meteorological analogy to war, N-nation problem, Tolstoyan view of war.

**"Counting is an antiseptic against prejudice"**
- Appears in Berreby (2014), Nautilus, attributed to Richardson.
- NOT verified verbatim in any primary source despite exhaustive search.
- Most likely from Korner (1996) "The Pleasures of Counting" Ch. 8-9 (not accessible).
- **Use with attribution to Berreby/Nautilus, or substitute the verified preface quote.**

### Deadly Quarrels Key Numbers
- 2 wars at magnitude 7 (WWI, WWII)
- 7 wars at magnitude 6 (Taiping, US Civil War, La Plata, Bolshevik sequel, Chinese Communist, Spanish Civil War, Indian communal riots)
- 315 conflicts magnitude 2.5+
- Two World Wars = ~36M deaths = ~60% of ALL quarrel deaths 1820-1950
- Only 12 of 94 two-participant wars had no shared border
- Arms races preceded only 13 of 315 cases
- Religion: the one statistically significant factor

## 5. Power Law and Modern Validation

### Claims
- War sizes follow a power law (many small, few large)
- Same pattern as turbulence and earthquakes
- Validated by modern researchers (Johnson, Spagat, Clauset)

### Sources

**Spagat, M., van Weezel, S., Restrepo, D.J., Zheng, M. & Johnson, N.F. (2020). "Unifying casualty distributions within and across conflicts." Heliyon 6(10).**
- URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7475112/
- **Scraped successfully.** Open access.
- Key finding: power-law exponent ~2.5 within wars aggregates to ~1.5-1.7 across whole wars.

**Bohorquez, J.C., et al. (2009). "Common ecology quantifies human insurgency." Nature 462(7275): 911-914.**
- Used data from Afghanistan, Iraq, Colombia
- Found insurgent wars share common power-law patterns

**Clauset, A. (2018). "Trends and fluctuations in the severity of interstate wars." Science Advances 4(2).**
- Confirmed power-law with better data
- "Long Peace" (since 1946) would need another 100 years to be statistically significant

**Richardson, L.F. (1948). "Variation of the frequency of fatal quarrels with magnitude." JASA 43(244): 523-546.**
- The original power-law publication.

## 6. The Pivot from Meteorology to War

### Claims
- Discovered atmospheric diffusion work useful for chemical weapons
- Destroyed unpublished papers
- Shell shock / PTSD symptoms
- "Let x be the will to hate"

### Sources

**Berreby, D. (2014). "Cloudy With a Chance of War." Nautilus Issue 15.**
- URL: https://nautil.us/cloudy-with-a-chance-of-war-234998
- Local PDF in sources directory.
- Confirms: shell shock detail ("one of his children recalls Richardson screaming in terror at sudden loud noises"), "Let x be the will to hate" anecdote, "counting is an antiseptic against prejudice", closing line about thunderstorm.

**Vulpiani (2014).**
- "As a coherent pacifist, Richardson refused for his research about diffusion to be used for war purposes. For several years, he left the study of turbulence and is supposed to have destroyed some of his unpublished works to prevent military uses."

**Wikipedia (scraped via curl).**
- "According to Thomas Korner, the discovery that his meteorological work was of value to chemical weapons designers caused him to abandon his efforts in this field and destroy findings he had not yet published."

## 7. Eskdalemuir Observatory

### Claims
- Remote Scottish location, "bleak and humid solitude"
- Where Richardson developed turbulence observations

### Sources

**Hayes (2001) American Scientist.**
- Richardson described Eskdalemuir as "bleak and humid solitude"
- "when he was once asked if he had any hobbies, his answer was 'solitude'"

**Gold (1954) Royal Society obituary, p. 221.**
- "appointed Superintendent of the Meteorological and Magnetic Observatory at Eskdalemuir, an isolated place in Dumfriesshire intentionally far from any railway, 800 feet above sea level"
