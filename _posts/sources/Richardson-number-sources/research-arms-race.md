## Key Quote
"The equations are merely a description of what people would do if they did not stop to think." - Richardson (1960a), p. 12, Arms and Insecurity.

## Equations
dx/dt = ky - ax + g
dy/dt = lx - by + h

Variables: x,y = arms spending; k,l = reaction coefficients; a,b = fatigue coefficients; g,h = grievance terms.

## Stability
- ab > kl: stable (fatigue beats reaction)
- kl > ab: unstable (arms race escalates)

## WWI Application
- Period: 1908-1914 military expenditures
- Nations: 15, aggregated into Entente vs Central Powers
- Finding: positive instability coefficients = "dangerous type"
- Equilibrium in 1907-1908, then divergence

## Shell Shock
"One of his children recalls Richardson screaming in terror at sudden loud noises, and explaining that he had 'shell shock'" - Berreby (2014)

## What He Got Right
1. Action-reaction concept
2. Security dilemma (anticipated Jervis 1978)
3. Cooperation mitigates arms races (anticipated commercial peace literature)
4. "Designed as a deterrent sometimes acted instead as an irritant" (Richardson 1960a: 55)
5. Distinction between stable/unstable arms races
6. Power law distribution of war sizes (validated by Clauset 2018, Johnson & Spagat)

## What He Got Wrong
1. No explicit mechanism linking arms races to war
2. Nations as black boxes (no internal politics)
3. Fixed linear coefficients (real responses are non-linear)
4. Limited empirical evidence for the specific model
