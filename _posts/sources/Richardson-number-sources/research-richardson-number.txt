1. BIOGRAPHICAL OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Lewis Fry Richardson, FRS (11 October 1881 – 30 September 1953)

- English mathematician, physicist, meteorologist, psychologist, and pacifist
- Born Newcastle-upon-Tyne, youngest of seven children in a prosperous Quaker
  family (David Richardson ran a tanning/leather business)
- Education: Bootham School (Quaker boarding school, York); Durham College of
  Science; King's College, Cambridge (first-class degree in physics, 1903,
  taught by J.J. Thomson)
- At age 47 received doctorate in mathematical psychology from University of London
- Married Dorothy Garnett (1909), daughter of physicist William Garnett
- Adopted two sons and a daughter (1920-1927)
- Nephew: the actor Sir Ralph Richardson
- Great-nephew (through wife's family): Julian Hunt, later director general of
  the British Meteorological Office (1992-1997)

Career timeline:
- National Physical Laboratory (1903-04, 1907-09)
- University College Aberystwyth (1905-06)
- Chemist, National Peat Industries (1906-07)
- Sunbeam Lamp Company, lab manager (1909-12)
- Manchester College of Technology (1912-13)
- Meteorological Office, superintendent of Eskdalemuir Observatory (1913-16)
- Friends' Ambulance Unit in France, attached to 16th French Infantry
  Division (1916-19) — conscientious objector during WWI
- Meteorological Office at Benson, Oxfordshire (1919-20) — resigned when it
  was absorbed into the Air Ministry ("I do not like preparations for war")
- Head of Physics Dept, Westminster Training College (1920-29)
- Principal, Paisley Technical College (1929-40)
- Retired 1940 to devote full time to peace research until death in 1953

Elected Fellow of the Royal Society in 1926.

Sources: Wikipedia (Lewis Fry Richardson), Encyclopedia.com, Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

2. THE 1920 PAPER & ORIGIN OF THE RICHARDSON NUMBER
================================================================================

PAPER: "The Supply of Energy from and to Atmospheric Eddies"
Published: Proceedings of the Royal Society of London, Series A, Vol. 97,
           Issue 686, pp. 354-373 (1920)
DOI: 10.1098/rspa.1920.0039

The paper extends Osborne Reynolds's theory of the Criterion of Turbulence to
the case of work done by eddies acting as thermodynamic engines in a gravitating
atmosphere. Richardson introduced analogies to thermodynamics, including the
conception of "eddy-heat-per-mass" and its diminution when air expands.

KEY CONTRIBUTION: Richardson first conceptualized the Richardson number parameter
in this 1920 paper, where it emerged as a criterion for assessing energy transfer
and turbulence in stratified atmospheres. This foundational work laid the
groundwork for his influential 1922 book "Weather Prediction by Numerical Process."

FAMOUS VERSE (from summary of the paper, reprinted in the 1922 book, p. 66):

  "Big whorls have little whorls
   Which feed on their velocity,
   And little whorls have lesser whorls,
   And so on to viscosity."

[A play on Augustus De Morgan's poem Siphonaptera: "Great fleas have little
fleas upon their backs to bite 'em, / And little fleas have lesser fleas,
and so ad infinitum." De Morgan's lines in turn rework Jonathan Swift's 1733
"On Poetry: A Rapsody."]

COPYRIGHT STATUS: The 1920 paper is over 100 years old. The Royal Society
Publishing website returned HTTP 403 when attempting to access the full text,
indicating access restrictions remain in place for the digital version. The
paper's text itself is in the public domain in most jurisdictions by age.

Source: ADS Harvard (ui.adsabs.harvard.edu), Royal Society Publishing,
todayinsci.com quotations, Wikipedia

3. THE RICHARDSON NUMBER — TECHNICAL DETAILS
================================================================================

DEFINITION: The Richardson number (Ri) is a dimensionless number expressing the
ratio of buoyancy to flow shear in fluid dynamics:

  Ri = (buoyancy) / (flow shear) = (g/rho) * (d_rho/dz) / (du/dz)^2

where:
  g = local gravitational acceleration
  rho = mass density
  u = representative flow velocity
  z = depth/height

PHYSICAL MEANING:
- Ri << 1: buoyancy can be neglected; shear-driven turbulence dominates
- Ri >> 1: buoyancy dominates; insufficient kinetic energy to homogenize fluid
- Ri ~ 1: flow is buoyancy-driven; energy derives from potential energy

CRITICAL VALUE: Ri < 0.25 is a NECESSARY CONDITION for velocity shear to
overcome stratification. Below this threshold, mixing (turbulence) will
generally occur. When Ri is large, turbulent mixing is suppressed.

VARIANTS IN ATMOSPHERIC SCIENCE:
1. Flux Richardson number (Ri_f) — fundamental; ratio of buoyant production/
   suppression of turbulence kinetic energy to shear production
2. Gradient Richardson number (Ri_g) — obtained by K-theory approximation
3. Bulk Richardson number (Ri_b) — finite difference approximation

IN AVIATION (from SKYbrary):
- Used as rough measure of expected air turbulence
- LOWER Ri = HIGHER turbulence
- Typical range: 10 to 0.1; values below 1 indicate significant turbulence
- Component of the Graphical Turbulence Guidance product (NOAA Aviation
  Weather Center)

STABILITY CLASSIFICATION TABLE (from SKYbrary):
  Stable (Ri > 0.25):     No vertical mixing, winds weak, strong inversion
  Stable (0 < Ri < 0.25): Mechanical turbulence weakened by stable stratification
  Neutral (Ri = 0):       Mechanical turbulence only
  Unstable (-0.03<Ri<0):  Mechanical turbulence and convection
  Unstable (Ri < -0.04):  Convection predominant, strong vertical motion

IN THERMAL CONVECTION:
  Ri = g*beta*(T_hot - T_ref)*L / v^2
  Can also be expressed as: Ri = Gr / Re^2
  (Grashof number / Reynolds number squared)
  - Ri < 0.1: natural convection negligible
  - Ri > 10: forced convection negligible
  - 0.1 < Ri < 10: neither negligible (mixed convection)

IN OCEANOGRAPHY:
  Ri = N^2 / (du/dz)^2
  where N = Brunt-Vaisala frequency
  Used in Taylor-Goldstein equation for Kelvin-Helmholtz instability

Sources: Wikipedia (Richardson number), SKYbrary, Encyclopaedia Britannica

4. RICHARDSON'S PEACE RESEARCH — OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Richardson is considered the initiator (or co-initiator, with Quincy Wright and
Pitirim Sorokin) of the scientific analysis of conflict. He spent at least 35
years studying war, devoting all his time after retirement in 1940.

HIS APPROACH — The Meteorological Analogy:
Richardson viewed war NOT as rational/purposeful behavior (the Clausewitzian view)
but in "Tolstoyan fashion, as a massive phenomenon governed by forces akin to the
forces of nature, over which individuals have little or no control." He drew a
direct analogy to weather: individual weather events seem random but are governed
by physical laws. Similarly, wars might follow statistical patterns even if
individual conflicts seem unpredictable.

Key insight from meteorology: "events which seem to be governed by chance... are
in fact governed by laws and can be predicted IF enough information can be
processed." He once estimated 60,000 human computers would be needed to calculate
tomorrow's weather before it arrived.

THREE MAJOR STRANDS:
1. Mathematical models of arms races (differential equations)
2. Statistical analysis of all "deadly quarrels" from 1820 to 1950
3. Geographical/topological analysis of conflict (borders, contiguity)

Source: Encyclopedia.com (Anatol Rapoport article), Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

5. "THE MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY OF WAR" (1919)
================================================================================

PUBLICATION DETAILS:
- Written in 1919, while Richardson was serving with the Friends' Ambulance
  Unit in France during WWI
- A 50-page essay, privately printed at Richardson's own expense
- Cost: 35 pounds for 300 copies total
- Published by Hunt, London
- Not published in a major venue until 1935, when a summary appeared in
  Nature (Vol. 135, pp. 830-831, 18 May 1935)
- Richardson's address at time of Nature publication: 38 Main Road,
  Castlehead, Paisley

CONTENTS:
Richardson developed a mathematical model for describing the change in
animosity between two opposing sides during a war. He postulated that the
rate of increase of warlike activity of one nation depended on the current
activity of the opposing nation.

Variables:
- x: variable preparedness for war
- t: time
- k: "defence-coefficient" (positive, more or less constant)
- fatigue and expense coefficient (positive, moderately constant)
- dissatisfactions-with-treaties (which tend to provoke a breach of peace)

NOTABLE FEATURE: The first edition contains Bertrand Russell's 20-line
contribution titled "Problem: To Produce in Two Nations a Mutual Will to War"
(pp. 15-16).

KEY PRINCIPLE: Although Richardson proposed formal, mechanistic equations as
a model of international relations, he thought of the causes of war as
primarily psychological — "a mass psychology much simplified by the averaging
out of many opposing pressures, devoid of self-insight or foresight."

Richardson studied psychology as an external student of University College,
London, receiving a B.Sc. in 1929.

Sources: Nature (1935), Peter Harrington rare book listing (web search),
Encyclopedia.com, Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

6. ARMS RACE MODELS (ARMS AND INSECURITY, 1949/1960)
================================================================================

PUBLICATION: "Arms and Insecurity: A Mathematical Study of the Causes and
Origins of War" — edited by Nicolas Rashevsky and Ernesto Trucco; published
posthumously 1960, Pittsburgh: Boxwood Press; Chicago: Quadrangle Books.

"Generalized Foreign Politics" (1939) was the complete version of his
mathematical theory of war, published on the eve of WWII.

THE MODEL — Pair of differential equations for two rival states:

Three components of interaction:
1. MUTUAL STIMULATION: Each nation's rate of armament increase is positively
   proportional to the other's current expenditures
2. SELF-INHIBITION ("fatigue and expense"): Rate of change is negatively
   proportional to the already existing armaments burden
3. CONSTANT STIMULANTS: Grievances or ambitions (can be negative = "good will")
   - Cooperation (e.g., trade) interpreted as negative armaments expenditures

KEY THEORETICAL RESULTS:
- Depending on relative magnitudes of stimulation vs. inhibition parameters
  (NOT the grievance parameters), the system may be STABLE or UNSTABLE
- If stable: arms balance possible (arms control feasible)
- If unstable: no balance possible; system must move toward either total
  disarmament/cooperation or runaway escalation/war
- Initial conditions determine which direction

EMPIRICAL TESTING:
- Good fit to armaments buildup of Central Powers vs. Entente (1908-1914)
  (though many free parameters limited the impressiveness of this)
- Using British post-WWI disarmament rates and German pre-WWII rearmament
  rates, Richardson estimated parameters were "well within the region of
  instability"
- Pre-WWI initial conditions were "touch and go" — possibly just slightly
  lower armaments or slightly more trade would have pushed toward united
  Europe instead of world war

N-NATION PROBLEM:
Richardson also posed the N-nation problem. Found that in a three-nation
arms race, the situation can be STABLE for each pair separately but
UNSTABLE for all three taken together — potentially relevant to nuclear
proliferation.

FAMOUS QUOTE: "The equations are merely a description of what people would
do if they did not stop to think." (Arms and Insecurity, p. 12)

Sources: Encyclopedia.com (Rapoport), Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

7. STATISTICS OF DEADLY QUARRELS (1960)
================================================================================

PUBLICATION: Edited by Quincy Wright and C. C. Lienau; published posthumously
1960, Pittsburgh: Boxwood Press. 373 pages.

DATA SCOPE: All "deadly quarrels" (defined as any quarrel causing death to
humans) from approximately 1820 to 1950. Includes murders, banditries,
mutinies, insurrections, and wars — but NOT accidents, natural calamities,
or indirect deaths from war-related famine/disease.

THE LOGARITHMIC SCALE:
- Richardson classified all conflicts by MAGNITUDE = log10(number of deaths)
- Single murder = magnitude 0 (log10(1) = 0)
- Small riot (~10 dead) = magnitude 1
- WWI and WWII = magnitude 7
- Rationale: casualty totals rarely known precisely, but logarithm usually
  estimable within +/- 0.5

MAJOR FINDINGS:

A) Distribution of war sizes:
- War sizes follow a POWER LAW (Pareto distribution): many small conflicts,
  few large ones
- The two World Wars alone account for ~36 million deaths = ~60% of ALL
  quarrel deaths in the 130-year period
- Next largest category: magnitude-0 events (1-3 dead) = 9.7 million deaths
- The 315 recorded wars + thousands of intermediate quarrels produced less
  than 25% of deaths

B) Seven magnitude-6 wars (500,000 to 2,000,000 dead):
  1. Taiping Rebellion (1851-1864)
  2. North American Civil War (1861-1865)
  3. Great War in La Plata (1865-1870)
  4. Sequel to Bolshevik Revolution (1918-1920)
  5. First Chinese-Communist War (1927-1936)
  6. Spanish Civil War (1936-1939)
  7. Communal riots in the Indian Peninsula (1946-1948)

C) Temporal patterns:
- Number of international wars per year follows a POISSON DISTRIBUTION
  (wars are effectively random events)
- Same pattern for "outbreaks of peace" (war endings)
- Similar Poisson pattern for gang murders in Chicago and Shanghai
- "The collection as a whole does not indicate any trend towards more, nor
  towards fewer, fatal quarrels."
- Some slight evidence of "contagion": ongoing war may increase probability
  of new war starting

D) Geographic factors:
- Of 94 international wars with two participants, only 12 had no shared border
- War is "mostly a neighborhood affair"
- Mean number of neighbors per state: ~6 (Richardson proved this geometrically
  using Euler's polyhedron relation)
- Notable: no examples of four countries meeting at a single point (unlike
  Four Corners in the US) — attributed to warfare shaping boundaries

E) Causal factors tested — mostly NEGATIVE results:
- Arms races: evidence found in only 13 of 315 cases
- Common language (Esperanto hope): NOT supported
- Economic indicators: explained only ~10% of causes — contradicts Marxist
  theory (though one reviewer noted the data could show economics contributed
  to 2/3 of causes)
- International trade: possible (weak) pacifying influence
- Common government: possible (weak) pacifying influence
- Armed might as deterrent: NOT significant
- Collective security: NOT significant

F) Religion — the ONE significant factor:
- Nations differing in religion are more likely to fight
- Christian nations participated in above-average number of hostilities,
  especially against Islamic nations
- "There were more wars between Christians and Moslems than would be
  expected from their populations, if religious differences had not tended
  to instigate quarrels between them."

G) Language findings:
- Spanish speakers tended to war against one another more than expected
- Chinese speakers fought against one another LESS than expected

H) Government effects:
- Countries under similar governments less likely to fight each other
  (precursor to democratic peace theory)
- Nationalism reduces chances of civil war while increasing chances of
  international war
- On world government: "There are arguments both ways."

I) Contagion: Fighting is "infectious" — neighbors of belligerent countries
   likely to be involved.

J) Scale observation: the larger the war, the more exponentially deadly —
   a warning against WWIII.

PREFACE QUOTE: "There is in the world a great deal of brilliant, witty
political discussion which leads to no settled convictions. My aim has been
different: namely to examine a few notions by quantitative techniques in the
hope of reaching a reliable answer."

CRITICAL RECEPTION (mixed):
- Paul Kecskemeti, Science (1960): "monumental" and "an important landmark"
- Philip C. Sagi, Annals of AAPSS (1961): "valuable addition to sociology
  of conflict"
- JASA anonymous reviewer (1961): "establish Richardson as an important
  precursor... in the mathematical analysis of conflict"
- Ian Sutherland, JRSS (1962): "remarkable pioneering achievement in a field
  which many would have regarded as not amenable to mathematical treatment"
- David Gold, ASR (1962): "one cannot help but be impressed by his bold
  imagination... Nevertheless, this work is science in the most superficial state."
- Amitai Etzioni, AJS (1962): "poor" use of mathematical models in sociology

Sources: Wikipedia (Statistics of Deadly Quarrels), American Scientist
(Brian Hayes, 2002), Encyclopedia.com, Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

8. THE RICHARDSON SCALE OF DEADLY QUARRELS
================================================================================

A scale devised by Richardson to assign a magnitude to events such as wars.
The magnitude = base-10 logarithm of the number of deaths.

EXAMPLES:
- Magnitude 0: Single murder (1 death)
- Magnitude 1: Small riot (~10 deaths)
- Magnitude 2: Terror campaign (~100 deaths)
- Magnitude 3: ~1,000 deaths
- Magnitude 4: ~10,000 deaths
- Magnitude 5: ~100,000 deaths
- Magnitude 6: ~1,000,000 deaths (seven conflicts identified 1820-1950)
- Magnitude 7: ~10,000,000 deaths (WWI and WWII only)

RICHARDSON'S LIST includes 315 conflicts of magnitude 2.5+ (roughly 300+ deaths).

The analogy to the Richter scale for earthquakes is deliberate — both use
logarithmic magnitudes to span enormous ranges. The comparison is not just
mathematical: Richardson saw wars as following statistical patterns analogous
to earthquakes, where individual events are unpredictable but the overall
distribution is lawful.

PRACTICAL BENEFIT: Using logarithms smooths over uncertainties in casualty
data. Richardson illustrated this with three estimates of Union army deaths
in the American Civil War (359,528; 279,376; 166,623) — the logarithms
(5.6, 5.4, 5.2) "bring out their substantial agreement."

BIOGRAPHICAL NOTE (from sizes.com): Richardson was a member of the Society of
Friends who served as an ambulance driver in France during WWI. When he learned
his meteorological ideas were being used to estimate the area over which
released poison gas would be effective, he abandoned the subject and destroyed
his unpublished papers.

Source: sizes.com, American Scientist (Hayes 2002), Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

9. POWER LAW FINDINGS AND MODERN VALIDATION
================================================================================

RICHARDSON'S ORIGINAL FINDING:
Richardson discovered that the distribution of war sizes follows a power law:
the frequency of wars of size x is proportional to x^(-alpha), where alpha is
a constant. This is sometimes called "Richardson's Law."

Key early publication: "Variation of the frequency of fatal quarrels with
magnitude" — Journal of the American Statistical Association 43(244): 523-546
(1948).

MODERN VALIDATION — Key researchers:

A) Erik-Lars Cederman (2003):
- "Modeling the Size of Wars: From Billiard Balls to Sandpiles" (American
  Political Science Review)
- Sought to explain Richardson's findings; confirmed power-law pattern

B) Neil F. Johnson & Michael Spagat — Key collaboration:
- "Common ecology quantifies human insurgency" — Bohorquez, Gourley, Dixon,
  Spagat & Johnson, Nature 462(7275): 911-914 (2009)
  * Used data from Afghanistan, Iraq, Colombia
  * Showed insurgent wars share common power-law patterns
  * Proposed unified model of human insurgency
  * Showed both individual event sizes within wars AND whole war sizes follow
    power laws

- "Simple mathematical law benchmarks human confrontations" — Johnson et al.,
  Scientific Reports 3: 3463 (2013)

- "Fundamental patterns and predictions of event size distributions in modern
  wars and terrorist campaigns" — Spagat, Johnson & van Weezel, PLoS ONE
  13(10) (2018)

C) Aaron Clauset (2018):
- "Trends and fluctuations in the severity of interstate wars" — Science
  Advances 4(2)
- Confirmed power-law distribution with better data and statistical tools
- Consistent with constant hazard of interstate war
- Concluded the "Long Peace" (since 1946) would have to last another 100
  years before it could be considered statistically significant

D) UNIFYING STUDY — Spagat, van Weezel, Restrepo, Zheng & Johnson (2020):
Published in Heliyon 6(10), PMC7475112

KEY NUMERICAL FINDINGS:
- Power-law exponent for WHOLE WARS: alpha ~ 1.5 to 1.7
- Power-law exponent for EVENTS WITHIN individual wars: beta ~ 2.5
- These two values are CONSISTENT with each other: aggregating casualties
  from individual events (with beta ~ 2.5) naturally produces the flatter
  distribution (alpha ~ 1.5-1.7) for whole wars
- This unifies the micro and macro scales of human conflict

MECHANISM EXPLANATION:
Some individual wars have low beta values (lots of large events relative to
small ones). Combined with a lognormal distribution of event counts per war,
this creates a non-negligible number of very large wars that fill the right
tail of the whole-war distribution, flattening it and reducing alpha below
the within-war beta.

ADDITIONAL RESEARCH:
- Patrick Meier (iRevolutions, 2011) tested whether conflict EVENTS (not just
  casualties) follow power laws, and whether casualties from natural disasters
  do as well
- Found strong evidence for power laws in disaster casualty distributions
  (globally and by continent except North America and non-EU Europe)
- Found slopes for disaster casualty power laws are much smaller than for
  modern wars and terrorism

Sources: PMC article (2020), iRevolutions (2011), Nature (2009), various
web search results

10. RICHARDSON'S OTHER CONTRIBUTIONS
================================================================================

A) FRACTALS AND THE COASTLINE PARADOX:
While searching for a relation between probability of war and common border
length, Richardson discovered that measured border/coastline lengths INCREASE
WITHOUT LIMIT as the unit of measurement shrinks. E.g., Spain-Portugal border
was quoted as 987 or 1,214 km depending on measurement method.

This is now known as the "Richardson effect" and the "coastline paradox."
Richardson identified a value (between 1 and 2) describing how observed
complexity changes with measurement detail — this became the model for
FRACTAL DIMENSION. Benoit Mandelbrot discovered Richardson's work and used it
as a starting point for his celebrated 1967 paper "How Long Is the Coast of
Britain?" and later his theory of fractals.

Published posthumously: "The problem of contiguity: An appendix to Statistics
of Deadly Quarrels" — General Systems Yearbook 6: 139-186 (1961)

B) NUMERICAL WEATHER PREDICTION:
"Weather Prediction by Numerical Process" (1922, Cambridge University Press)
founded the science of numerical weather prediction. His famous "forecast
factory" fantasy envisioned 64,000 human computers. His actual trial forecast
for May 20, 1910 failed (predicted 145 hPa rise in 6 hours) but Peter Lynch
showed this was due to not applying smoothing; with smoothing, the forecast
was essentially accurate. When ENIAC produced the first computer weather
forecast in 1950, Richardson called it "an enormous scientific advance."

C) RICHARDSON EXTRAPOLATION:
A sequence acceleration method for numerical analysis, still widely used.

D) MODIFIED RICHARDSON ITERATION:
Method for solving systems of linear equations.

E) SONAR/ECHOLOCATION PATENTS:
In April 1912, after the Titanic sinking, Richardson filed a patent for
iceberg detection using acoustic echolocation in air. A month later, a
similar patent for echolocation in water — anticipating sonar by 6 years.

F) ATMOSPHERIC DIFFUSION:
His 1926 paper on atmospheric diffusion (Proc. Roy. Soc. A, 110(756):
709-737) remains his most cited work, with well over 1000 citations on Web
of Science, still receiving 42 citations in the first 7 months of 2018 alone.

Sources: Wikipedia, Springer (Gleditsch 2020)

12. THE "COUNTING IS AN ANTISEPTIC AGAINST PREJUDICE" QUOTE — SEARCH RESULTS
================================================================================

EXTENSIVE SEARCH CONDUCTED across multiple databases and sources. The exact
phrase "counting is an antiseptic against prejudice" was NOT found verbatim in
any of the following:
- Wikipedia articles on Richardson and Statistics of Deadly Quarrels
- American Scientist article by Brian Hayes (2002)
- Encyclopedia.com article (by Anatol Rapoport)
- Springer volume "Lewis Fry Richardson: His Intellectual Legacy" (Gleditsch, 2020)
- todayinsci.com quotations collection (5 verified Richardson quotes)
- Nature (1935) summary
- Google Scholar and general web searches with multiple variants

CLOSEST VERIFIED SENTIMENTS:
Richardson expressed the idea that quantitative/statistical analysis is a
defense against bias and prejudice in the study of war in these documented ways:

(a) "There is in the world a great deal of brilliant, witty political
discussion which leads to no settled convictions. My aim has been different:
namely to examine a few notions by quantitative techniques in the hope of
reaching a reliable answer." — Preface, Statistics of Deadly Quarrels

(b) Brian Hayes (American Scientist, 2002) summarized Richardson's philosophy:
"One defense against such distortions is the statistical method of gathering
data about many wars from many sources, in the hope that at least some of the
biases will balance out and true patterns will emerge."

(c) The title "The Pleasures of Counting" by T.W. Korner (1996, Cambridge UP),
which devotes Chapters 8-9 to Richardson, may contain this or a similar quote
in the original text. The chapter titles are "A Quaker Mathematician" (pp.
159-193) and "Richardson on War" (pp. 194-227). This book would be the most
likely secondary source for such a quote.

RECOMMENDATION: The quote may exist in the original text of Statistics of
Deadly Quarrels or Arms and Insecurity (both available at archive.org). It may
also appear in Korner's "The Pleasures of Counting." For a blog post, it
would be safest to either verify from the original text or use one of the
documented quotes above that express the same sentiment.

NOTE: The spirit of the quote is entirely consistent with Richardson's
philosophy. His entire approach to peace research was predicated on the idea
that counting, measuring, and applying mathematics to war would overcome the
emotional and ideological biases that plague political discussion.
